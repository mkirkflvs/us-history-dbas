Standard 7.01
President Johnson's Great Society
Think About It
LBJ pushed through more landmark legislation in two years than any president since FDR. What do you think made that historical moment possible — and what were its limits?

🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
President Johnson signs the Civil Rights Act of 1964 — after 100 years, the federal government finally outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. Library of Congress.
Civil Rights Legislation
  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: banned discrimination in public places and employment based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965: prohibited voter suppression tactics — literacy tests, poll taxes — with federal oversight in states with history of discrimination
  • 24th Amendment (1964): abolished poll taxes in federal elections
  • Fair Housing Act (1968): banned discrimination in the sale and rental of housing
Need a starting point?
"The Civil Rights Act of 1964 mattered because it finally…"
War on Poverty
  • Economic Opportunity Act (1964): created Job Corps, Head Start, VISTA — invested in education and job training for poor Americans
  • Medicare and Medicaid (1965): federal health insurance for elderly and low-income Americans — both still exist today
  • Elementary and Secondary Education Act (1965): first major federal funding for K-12 public education
  • Food Stamp Program expanded — provided nutrition assistance to poor families nationwide
Need a starting point?
"The War on Poverty programs tried to address poverty by…"
Comparing to the New Deal
  • Both dramatically expanded the federal government's role in citizens' welfare
  • New Deal focused on economic recovery — jobs and safety nets; Great Society focused on civil rights and equal opportunity
  • Great Society explicitly addressed racial inequality — the New Deal had largely excluded Black Americans in practice
  • Both faced strong conservative opposition claiming government overreach — and both left programs that exist today
Need a starting point?
"The Great Society was similar to and different from the New Deal in that…"
Let's Go Deeper
LBJ passed landmark civil rights legislation — but also dramatically escalated the Vietnam War. How do you explain a president who simultaneously advanced civil rights and divided the country so deeply over the war?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the major accomplishments of the Great Society. Which program do you think had the most lasting impact — and what were the limits of what legislation could actually achieve?

Standard 7.02
Key Figures of the Civil Rights Movement
Think About It
When we talk about the Civil Rights Movement, we often talk about one man. But thousands of people built the movement. Who else should we be talking about?

SCLC & SNCC
  • SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference): MLK's organization — nonviolent direct action, church networks, media strategy
  • SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee): young activists, more confrontational — led voter registration in the Deep South
  • Freedom Riders (1961): CORE and SNCC activists tested desegregation on interstate buses — faced violent mobs in Alabama
  • SNCC field workers risked their lives registering voters in Mississippi; Robert Moses organized in the most dangerous counties
Need a starting point?
"The SCLC and SNCC both fought for civil rights but differed in that…"
Malcolm X & Black Power
  • Malcolm X: Nation of Islam minister — rejected nonviolence and integration; advocated Black self-defense and self-determination
  • "By any means necessary" — represented the frustration of those who saw nonviolent change as too slow
  • Black Power (1966): Stokely Carmichael's call for Black political and economic self-determination
  • Black Panther Party (1966): Bobby Seale and Huey Newton — community self-defense, free breakfast programs, confronted police brutality directly
Need a starting point?
"Malcolm X and the Black Power movement challenged the nonviolent approach by arguing…"
Women's Leadership
  • Fannie Lou Hamer: Mississippi sharecropper turned voting rights activist — her 1964 testimony at the Democratic convention was nationally televised
  • Rosa Parks: NAACP secretary and trained activist who carefully chose when to act — her arrest launched the Montgomery Bus Boycott
  • Ella Baker: organized SNCC — believed movement power came from grassroots people, not charismatic leaders
  • Diane Nash: organized the Nashville sit-ins and Freedom Rides — one of the movement's most effective strategists
Need a starting point?
"Women's leadership in the Civil Rights Movement was essential because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Ella Baker argued that the movement's power came from ordinary people, not charismatic leaders. MLK's approach centered on his own public leadership. Who do you think was right about where the movement's power came from?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the range of people and strategies in the Civil Rights Movement. Which figure or organization do you think made the most important contribution — and why? Make a case for your choice.

Standard 7.02
Protests and Legislative Turning Points
Think About It
Nonviolent protest worked not because protesters didn't get hurt — they absolutely did. It worked because it exposed the violence of the system in a way television brought into every American living room. How do you think that strategy was designed to work?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Bloody Sunday at the Edmund Pettus Bridge, Selma, Alabama, March 7, 1965 — state troopers attacked peaceful marchers; the images broadcast nationally helped push the Voting Rights Act to passage. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Selma to Montgomery march route, 1965 — 54 miles, three attempts, and national television coverage that changed the political equation and led to the Voting Rights Act. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Bloody Sunday, Edmund Pettus Bridge, Selma, March 7, 1965 — state troopers attacked peaceful marchers. The images broadcast nationally that night shocked the country and gave LBJ the political cover to pass the Voting Rights Act. Library of Congress.
Boycotts & Sit-Ins
  • Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-56): 381 days — 40,000 Black residents refused to ride city buses; ended with Supreme Court ruling bus segregation unconstitutional
  • Greensboro Sit-Ins (Feb 1960): four Black college students at a Woolworth's counter — within weeks the tactic spread to 50 cities
  • Freedom Rides (1961): interracial groups rode buses across the South — attacked in Alabama; federal government forced to act
  • Birmingham Campaign (1963): Bull Connor used fire hoses and police dogs against peaceful protesters — images seen worldwide
Need a starting point?
"The Montgomery Bus Boycott was significant because it showed…"
Marches & Confrontations
  • March on Washington (August 28, 1963): 250,000 people — MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech; pushed Kennedy to submit civil rights legislation
  • Freedom Summer (1964): 1,000 volunteers registered Black voters in Mississippi — three workers murdered
  • Selma to Montgomery March (1965): Bloody Sunday — troopers attacked marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge; national outrage drove the Voting Rights Act forward
  • Poor People's Campaign (1968): MLK was organizing this when he was assassinated in Memphis, Tennessee
Need a starting point?
"The March on Washington mattered because…"
Laws That Changed America
  • Civil Rights Act (1964): passed after JFK's assassination — Johnson used national grief and political skill to push it through
  • Voting Rights Act (1965): passed after Selma's Bloody Sunday — direct line from protest to legislation
  • 24th Amendment (1964): abolished poll taxes in federal elections — same year as the Civil Rights Act
  • Fair Housing Act (1968): passed in the week after MLK's assassination — tragedy repeatedly preceded legislation
Need a starting point?
"The connection between protest and legislation in this era showed that…"
Let's Go Deeper
Every major Civil Rights legislative victory came in the wake of a crisis or tragedy. What does that tell you about how change actually happens in American democracy — and what does it demand of the people pushing for it?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Choose one protest event and explain how it worked — the strategy, the risk, and what changed as a result. Then step back: how did the movement turn protest into law?

Standard 7.03
Other Social Movements of the Era
Think About It
The Civil Rights Movement inspired other groups to organize and demand their own rights. What do you think it takes for a group to decide the time has come — and what can they learn from movements that came before?
Historical Image

The Women's Strike for Equality, New York City, August 26, 1970 — 50,000 women marched on the 50th anniversary of the 19th Amendment, demanding equal rights in work and society. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

The Chicano Movement
  • UFW: Cesar Chavez and Dolores Huerta organized Mexican-American farm workers — grape boycott (1965) won the first union contract for farm workers
  • Student walkouts (1968): East Los Angeles high school students walked out demanding better education — the "blowouts"
  • La Raza Unida: political party to represent Mexican-American interests; elected local officials in Texas
  • Brown Berets: Chicano activist group inspired by Black Panthers — community self-defense and political education
Need a starting point?
"The Chicano Movement fought for…"
American Indian Movement (AIM)
  • AIM founded 1968: addressed police brutality against Native Americans in urban areas
  • Occupation of Alcatraz (1969-71): 89 Native Americans claimed the island under an 1868 treaty — brought national attention to Native rights
  • Wounded Knee Occupation (1973): AIM activists occupied the site of the 1890 massacre — 71-day standoff with federal marshals
  • Indian Self-Determination Act (1975): gave tribes more control over their own education and programs on reservations
Need a starting point?
"The American Indian Movement was significant because it demanded…"
The Women's Movement
  • Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique (1963): challenged the idea women were fulfilled by domesticity — sparked a generation
  • NOW (National Organization for Women, 1966): demanded equal pay, equal opportunity, and reproductive rights
  • Title IX (1972): banned sex discrimination in any educational program receiving federal funds — transformed women's sports
  • ERA (Equal Rights Amendment): passed Congress in 1972; failed ratification — conservative opposition led by Phyllis Schlafly
Need a starting point?
"The women's movement of the 1960s and 70s challenged the idea that…"
Let's Go Deeper
All these movements were inspired by the Civil Rights Movement but faced their own specific obstacles. What did they share in terms of goals and strategies — and what made each struggle distinct?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare two of these movements. What did they share in terms of goals and strategies, and what was unique to each group's struggle?

Standard 7.04
Supreme Court Cases and Civil Rights
Think About It
The Supreme Court's job is to interpret the Constitution — but every ruling reflects a set of values about who deserves protection. What should guide the Court when it expands rights the Constitution doesn't explicitly mention?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The U.S. Supreme Court — in the 1960s, the Warren Court issued a series of landmark rulings that dramatically expanded individual rights and civil liberties. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The U.S. Supreme Court — in the 1960s, the Warren Court issued landmark rulings that dramatically expanded the rights of individuals against government and institutional power. Library of Congress.
Student Rights
  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954): segregated schools declared unconstitutional — overturned Plessy v. Ferguson
  • Tinker v. Des Moines (1969): students wearing anti-war armbands — Court ruled students don't "shed constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate"
  • In re Gault (1967): juveniles accused of crimes have the right to an attorney and due process
  • West Virginia v. Barnette (1943): students cannot be compelled to salute the flag — compelled speech violates the First Amendment
Need a starting point?
"Tinker v. Des Moines mattered because it established that…"
Suspects' Rights
  • Mapp v. Ohio (1961): evidence from illegal searches cannot be used in court — the "exclusionary rule" applied to states
  • Gideon v. Wainwright (1963): any person charged with a felony has the right to an attorney, even if they can't afford one
  • Miranda v. Arizona (1966): police must inform suspects of their rights before interrogation — "Miranda warnings" became standard
  • Escobedo v. Illinois (1964): suspects have the right to an attorney during police questioning, not just at trial
Need a starting point?
"Miranda v. Arizona changed law enforcement because…"
Privacy & Reproductive Rights
  • Griswold v. Connecticut (1965): Connecticut banned contraceptives — Court found a "right to privacy" in the Constitution; married couples cannot be denied contraception
  • Loving v. Virginia (1967): laws banning interracial marriage declared unconstitutional — struck down 16 states' laws
  • Roe v. Wade (1973): extended right to privacy to include abortion decisions in the first trimester
  • Eisenstadt v. Baird (1972): extended contraception rights to unmarried people — not just married couples
Need a starting point?
"Griswold v. Connecticut was significant because it established a constitutional right to…"
Let's Go Deeper
The Warren Court dramatically expanded individual rights — but critics argued it was "legislating from the bench." What is the proper role of the Supreme Court in a democracy? Should unelected judges be able to expand rights the Constitution doesn't explicitly name?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Pick two Supreme Court cases from the 1960s-70s and explain what right each one protected and why it mattered. Then think about the bigger pattern: what does the Warren Court's record tell you about how rights expand in America?
