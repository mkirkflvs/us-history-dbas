Standard 7.01
LBJ's Great Society: How Much Can Laws Do?
Think About It
LBJ passed more landmark legislation in two years than any president since FDR. What makes a political moment like that possible, and why can't it last?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
President Johnson signs the Civil Rights Act of 1964 — after 100 years, the federal government finally outlawed discrimination based on race. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Levittown — the segregated prosperity of the 1950s is exactly what LBJ's Great Society was designed to address. Library of Congress.
Lyndon Johnson used Kennedy's assassination, an overwhelming 1964 election mandate, and genuine personal commitment to civil rights to push through a legislative avalanche: Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act, Medicare, Medicaid, federal education funding. It was the high-water mark of liberal governance. Then Vietnam swallowed the money and the backlash swallowed the votes.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Laws
Civil Rights Act (1964) banned discrimination in public accommodations and employment. Voting Rights Act (1965) banned voter suppression. Medicare and Medicaid created health coverage for elderly and poor Americans still operating today.
›
Step 2
The War on Poverty
Head Start, Job Corps, federal education funding, programs designed to create opportunity, not just remove barriers. Some worked; some didn't. All were eventually cut back.
›
Step 3
The Contradiction
LBJ escalated Vietnam while building the Great Society. The war consumed money and political capital. The backlash began almost immediately. Nixon won in 1968 partly by opposing everything Johnson had done.
🔗
Real World Connection
Medicare and Medicaid cover over 130 million Americans today. Head Start still operates. Every argument you hear today about government programs, from Medicare debates to welfare policy, is still fighting LBJ's war. Should government create the floor? Or does that undercut the individual? The 1960s didn't settle it.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Great Society's most lasting impact was... but its biggest limitation was..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
LBJ passed more civil rights legislation than anyone, and also escalated Vietnam. How do you hold both of those things at once when evaluating his legacy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the major accomplishments of the Great Society. Which do you think mattered most, and what were the limits of what legislation could actually achieve?

Standard 7.02
The People Who Built the Movement
Think About It
We talk about the Civil Rights Movement like it had one leader. But it had thousands. Why does the official story usually center on one person?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
MLK at the March on Washington, August 28, 1963 — 250,000 people. But Bayard Rustin and thousands of others organized it. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The House of Representatives, 1868 — during Reconstruction, Black men held legislative office across the South. The Civil Rights Movement was reclaiming that lost ground. Library of Congress.
The Civil Rights Movement was not MLK's movement, it was a massive, multi-strategy, multigenerational effort involving thousands of ordinary people who took real risks. MLK was brilliant and essential, but so were Ella Baker, Fannie Lou Hamer, Diane Nash, SNCC field workers registering voters in Mississippi, and Malcolm X articulating a Black American anger that didn't fit polite narratives.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Mainstream
MLK and SCLC used nonviolent direct action, media strategy, and church networks. They made the movement legible to white moderates and gave politicians cover to act.
›
Step 2
The Grassroots
SNCC operated at the ground level, student sit-ins, Freedom Rides, voter registration in Mississippi's most dangerous counties. Ella Baker believed the movement's strength came from ordinary people, not leaders.
›
Step 3
The Radical
Malcolm X articulated a fury that nonviolent protests couldn't contain. Black Power demanded self-determination, not integration. The presence of a more radical alternative actually made MLK's demands seem more reasonable to white politicians.
🔗
Real World Connection
Think about any movement you know of today. There's always a "respectable" face that gets media attention, a grassroots organizing base that does the actual work, and a more radical wing that sets the outer edge of demands. The Civil Rights Movement had all three working simultaneously.

◈ Let's Go Deeper
Ella Baker argued that the movement's power came from ordinary people, not charismatic leaders. MLK's approach centered on his own public leadership. Who was right, and does it matter?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the range of people and strategies in the Civil Rights Movement. Who do you think made the most important contribution, and why?

Standard 7.02
How Protest Becomes Law
Think About It
Nonviolent protest worked not because it was comfortable, people got beaten, arrested, and killed. It worked because it forced the violence of the system into living rooms across America. How was that strategy designed to work?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Bloody Sunday, Edmund Pettus Bridge, March 7, 1965 — state troopers attacked peaceful marchers. The footage that night moved the country and passed the Voting Rights Act. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Selma to Montgomery march, 1965 — 54 miles, three attempts, and national television coverage that changed everything. Library of Congress.
The genius of the Civil Rights Movement's nonviolent strategy was forcing a confrontation that the opposition couldn't win in the court of public opinion. When Bull Connor turned fire hoses on children in Birmingham, or state troopers beat marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge, the images broadcast nationwide were more powerful than any speech. Protest made injustice undeniable.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Tactics
Montgomery Bus Boycott (381 days), Greensboro sit-ins (spread to 50 cities in weeks), Freedom Rides, each tactic put bodies on the line to force a confrontation with injustice.
›
Step 2
The Crisis Points
Birmingham (1963): Bull Connor's fire hoses. Selma (1965): Bloody Sunday. Both produced images that shocked the nation and gave Congress the political cover to act.
›
Step 3
The Laws
Civil Rights Act (1964) came after Birmingham. Voting Rights Act (1965) came after Selma. Every major law was passed in the wake of a crisis. The pattern is uncomfortable but clear.
🔗
Real World Connection
The connection between what happens in the streets and what happens in legislatures is direct, but only when the moral stakes are undeniable and visible. Think about how video footage of police violence today follows a similar logic to the Selma footage in 1965.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The civil rights strategy of nonviolent direct action worked because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Every major civil rights law came in the wake of a crisis or tragedy. What does that tell you about how change actually happens in American democracy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Choose one protest event and explain how it worked, the strategy, the risk, and what changed as a result.

Standard 7.03
Other Voices, Other Movements
Think About It
The Civil Rights Movement showed that organized people could force a country to live up to its promises. Who else was watching and taking notes?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Women's Strike for Equality, New York City, August 26, 1970 — 50,000 marched on the 50th anniversary of the 19th Amendment. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Jim Crow — the Civil Rights Movement gave every excluded group a template. They were all fighting the same system of enforced inequality, each in their own way. Library of Congress.
The Civil Rights Movement didn't just win rights for Black Americans, it gave a template to every other group that had been excluded from the American promise. Cesar Chavez and Dolores Huerta organized farm workers. AIM occupied Alcatraz and Wounded Knee. Betty Friedan and NOW demanded women's equality. Each movement borrowed tactics, built coalitions, and faced its own particular resistance.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Chicano Movement
The United Farm Workers grape boycott, supported by millions of consumers, eventually won union contracts for farm workers who had been treated as invisible. "Sí se puede" (Yes we can) came from here.
›
Step 2
AIM
The American Indian Movement occupied Alcatraz (1969) and Wounded Knee (1973), demanding recognition of treaty rights and sovereignty. It forced the country to confront the unfinished business of Native American history.
›
Step 3
The Women's Movement
The Feminine Mystique (1963), NOW (1966), Title IX (1972). Women weren't asking for new rights, they were demanding the existing ones actually apply to them. Title IX transformed women's sports and education.
🔗
Real World Connection
Title IX (1972) is why women's sports programs exist the way they do today. Before it, there were essentially no scholarships and minimal programs. The movement's victory is literally in every women's athletic scholarship, something most athletes take for granted without knowing the history behind it.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"These different movements were connected because they all... even though each faced its own specific..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
All of these movements were inspired by the Civil Rights Movement but faced their own unique obstacles. What did they share, and what made each struggle distinct?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare two of these movements. What did they share and what was unique to each? What did each accomplish?

Standard 7.04
The Supreme Court Expands Rights
Think About It
The Constitution is a document from 1787. How does a 200-year-old text protect rights that didn't exist when it was written?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Supreme Court building — in the 1960s, the Warren Court issued landmark rulings expanding rights for individuals against institutional power. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Protesters demanding rights — every Warren Court ruling was the legal form of what people were demanding in the streets. Library of Congress.
The Warren Court (1953-1969) interpreted the Constitution more expansively than any previous Court, finding rights the text didn't explicitly mention but that could be inferred from its principles. Students, suspects, and ordinary people got constitutional protections they'd never had before. Conservatives called it "legislating from the bench." Supporters called it the Constitution finally meaning what it said.
How It Connects
Step 1
Student & Civil Rights
Brown v. Board (1954) ended legal school segregation. Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) said students don't "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate." Rights don't stop at the school door.
›
Step 2
Criminal Justice
Gideon (1963): you have the right to a lawyer even if you can't afford one. Miranda (1966): police must tell you your rights before questioning. These changed daily police work permanently.
›
Step 3
Privacy
Griswold (1965) found a constitutional "right to privacy" that the text never mentioned, but that the Warren Court found implied in multiple amendments. It became the foundation for Roe v. Wade and much else.
🔗
Real World Connection
The Miranda warning, "You have the right to remain silent,", is in every crime show you've ever watched. Before 1966, it didn't exist. Everything you see on TV about police procedure was shaped by a Supreme Court ruling. Law school students still argue about whether the Warren Court was right.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Warren Court expanded constitutional rights by... which was controversial because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The Warren Court dramatically expanded individual rights, but critics argued it was "legislating from the bench." What is the proper role of the Supreme Court in a democracy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Pick two Warren Court cases and explain what right each one protected and why it mattered. Then look at the bigger pattern: how does the Constitution expand to cover new situations?
