Standard 6.01
Building a New World Order
Think About It
After the deadliest war in history, the world tried to build institutions that could prevent it from ever happening again. Why is that so hard?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Nuremberg Trials, 1945 — for the first time in history, national leaders were prosecuted for crimes against humanity. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
D-Day — the generation that won the war also built the post-war international order, determined never to let it happen again. Library of Congress.
The years immediately after WWII were genuinely optimistic: Nuremberg held war criminals accountable, the UN was founded, and the Marshall Plan started rebuilding Europe. But the Soviet Union and the U.S. were already competing for dominance, and within two years of V-E Day, Winston Churchill was describing an "Iron Curtain" dividing Europe.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Accountability
The Nuremberg Trials established that "crimes against humanity" are prosecutable under international law and that "following orders" is not a defense. Revolutionary principles, inconsistently applied.
›
Step 2
The Institutions
The United Nations was designed to be what the League of Nations wasn't: an organization with U.S. membership, real authority, and mechanisms to prevent war. It was imperfect, but it still exists.
›
Step 3
The Competition
The Truman Doctrine committed the U.S. to opposing communist expansion anywhere. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Western Europe. Both were about keeping countries in the American sphere, not just about altruism.
🔗
Real World Connection
The United Nations, the World Bank, the IMF: all created in the late 1940s, all still running today. The next time you hear "the international community," that infrastructure was built by people who'd just survived two world wars and were desperately trying to prevent a third.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The post-WWII world order was built on... with the goal of..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The Marshall Plan gave $12 billion to rebuild Western Europe. Was that generosity, or strategy? Does it matter which one it was if the outcome was good?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the three pillars of the post-WWII order. Nuremberg, the UN, and Truman's foreign policy. What were they trying to build, and how well did it work?

Standard 6.02
The 1950s: Golden Age or For Whom?
Think About It
People remember the 1950s as a simpler, better time. But better for whom?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Levittown, New York — the first mass-produced suburb. The American Dream of the 1950s came with a catch: Black veterans could not buy homes here. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
FDR Memorial breadline — the 1950s prosperity was defined by contrasting itself with what came before. For people excluded from the boom, that contrast meant less. Library of Congress.
The postwar boom was real: the GI Bill created a new middle class, suburbs exploded, and a consumer culture built on TV and credit cards was born. But this golden age was explicitly built to exclude: Black veterans were denied GI Bill benefits in practice, suburbs were explicitly segregated by race, and women were shoved back out of the jobs they'd proven they could do. The 1950s prosperity was also the 1950s pressure cooker.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Prosperity
The GI Bill sent 8 million veterans to college and funded millions of homes. The baby boom, suburbanization, and TV culture transformed American life. For white middle-class families, it genuinely was a golden age.
›
Step 2
The Exclusion
Black veterans were denied home loans in white suburbs, excluded from most GI Bill benefits in practice, and still subject to Jim Crow. Women were pushed back into domestic roles after wartime factory work.
›
Step 3
The Pressure
Conformity was enforced. McCarthyism punished dissent. Youth culture, rock and roll, the Beat Generation, was a reaction against suffocation. The 1950s contained the seeds of the 1960s.
🔗
Real World Connection
Nostalgia for the 1950s depends entirely on who you are. The era's prosperity was built partly on excluding people from it. That's worth thinking about whenever any era gets romanticized. "The good old days" weren't good for everyone.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The 1950s looked like prosperity and stability, but for many Americans it actually meant..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The 1950s are often called America's golden age. Who gets to remember it that way, and whose experience gets erased in that nostalgia?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe post-WWII prosperity, what it looked like and who it served. Then explain: is "golden age" a fair description?

Standard 6.03
McCarthyism: When Fear Becomes a Weapon
Think About It
A senator claimed he had a list of communists inside the U.S. government, and for years, nobody would call him a liar. What does it take for a democracy to let that happen?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
McCarthy at the Army-McCarthy hearings, 1954 — when Joseph Welch asked "Have you no sense of decency?" on live TV, McCarthy's power began to collapse. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Red Scare cartoon, 1919 — McCarthyism in the 1950s used the exact same playbook. Fear is a reusable political tool. Library of Congress.
Senator Joseph McCarthy never produced solid evidence for any of his accusations. He destroyed hundreds of careers based on rumors, associations, and political pressure. He was eventually stopped, but only after years of ruining lives. The mechanism was simple: accuse someone of communist sympathy, force them to either name others or be named themselves, and let the social pressure do the rest.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Tool
McCarthy weaponized the fear of Soviet espionage (which was real, the Rosenbergs were genuine Soviet spies) to target progressives, labor organizers, and anyone he wanted to silence.
›
Step 2
The Damage
The Hollywood Ten went to prison for refusing to name names. Hundreds of careers were destroyed. Artists, writers, and teachers lost jobs based on unproven accusations.
›
Step 3
The End
When McCarthy attacked the Army on live television in 1954, attorney Joseph Welch asked: "Have you no sense of decency?" The country was watching. McCarthy's spell broke.
🔗
Real World Connection
Think about how accusations spread on social media before evidence is verified. The mechanism isn't that different from McCarthyism, someone makes a claim, the crowd reacts, and by the time the truth emerges, the damage is done. The technology is new; the human dynamic isn't.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"McCarthyism succeeded for so long because... and it was finally stopped when..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Some people who were "McCarthyized" were actually Soviet spies or sympathizers. Does that make McCarthy's methods more acceptable, or does it not matter?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what McCarthyism was and how it worked. Then think about what it tells us about democracy's vulnerabilities under pressure.

Standard 6.04
The Forgotten War: Korea
Think About It
36,574 Americans died in the Korean War. Most Americans can't tell you much about it. Why do some wars get remembered and others get forgotten?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. Marines at Chosin Reservoir, December 1950 — outnumbered 8-to-1 in brutal cold, they fought their way out. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Wilson in 1919 — the post-WWI international order he helped build was directly tested in Korea. The UN Security Council authorized the response. Library of Congress.
Korea is called the "Forgotten War", sandwiched between the heroism of WWII and the trauma of Vietnam, it doesn't fit a clean narrative. It started as a communist invasion, escalated when China entered, and ended in a stalemate. The war established the Cold War template: fight for containment, not victory; limited war with limited goals.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Conflict
North Korea invaded South Korea in June 1950. The U.S. led a UN coalition that pushed back. MacArthur's brilliant Inchon landing reversed the war, then he pushed toward China. China entered with 300,000 troops.
›
Step 2
The Constitutional Moment
MacArthur publicly defied Truman's order not to expand the war against China. Truman fired him. The principle of civilian control of the military was upheld, a bigger deal than it sounds.
›
Step 3
The Stalemate
The war ended in 1953 almost exactly where it started, the 38th parallel. Korea is still divided today. It was a "win" in the sense that South Korea survived, but nobody felt like celebrating.
🔗
Real World Connection
Korea established the "limited war" concept, fighting to contain rather than defeat. Vietnam was the same idea, applied in a different place with catastrophically different results. Understanding Korea helps explain why Vietnam went so wrong.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Korean War mattered because it established... even though..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The Korean War ended almost exactly where it started. 36,000 Americans died. Was it worth it?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the Korean War, why it started, what happened, and what it accomplished. Then explain why Truman firing MacArthur was actually a bigger deal than most people realize.

Standard 6.05
The Cold War Heats Up
Think About It
Two countries with enough nuclear weapons to end civilization. Neither willing to use them. What keeps two superpowers from going to war when they hate each other that much?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Sputnik 1, October 4, 1957 — if the Soviets could orbit a satellite, they could deliver a nuclear warhead anywhere on Earth. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Nagasaki, 1945 — MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction) was the logic that kept the Cold War cold. Both sides knew using nuclear weapons meant their own destruction. Library of Congress.
MAD. Mutually Assured Destruction, was the grim logic that kept the Cold War cold. Both sides knew that using nuclear weapons meant their own destruction. So the competition played out in proxy wars, spy operations, the Space Race, and moments of terrifying near-miss, most notably the Cuban Missile Crisis, 13 days in October 1962 when the world came closer to nuclear war than ever before.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Arms Race
Both sides built thousands of nuclear warheads. Sputnik (1957) proved the Soviets could put a missile anywhere. The U.S. panicked and poured money into NASA, science education, and defense.
›
Step 2
The Brink
Soviet missiles in Cuba, 90 miles from Florida. Kennedy ordered a naval blockade. Khrushchev's ships headed for Cuba. For 13 days, the world waited. Both sides blinked simultaneously.
›
Step 3
The Lesson
Both Kennedy and Khrushchev made concessions to avoid nuclear war. Kennedy secretly agreed to remove missiles from Turkey; Khrushchev removed from Cuba. Compromise saved civilization.
🔗
Real World Connection
The "hotline" between Washington and Moscow was installed immediately after the Cuban Missile Crisis, because both sides realized they needed a direct communication channel to prevent accidental nuclear war. That's the origin of the red phone that shows up in every Cold War movie.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Cuban Missile Crisis showed that nuclear weapons changed international politics because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
During the missile crisis, both Kennedy and Khrushchev ignored military advisors urging escalation. What does that tell you about nuclear weapons as a tool of foreign policy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through Cold War escalation from Sputnik to the Cuban Missile Crisis. Focus on one moment and explain why it mattered so much.

Standard 6.06
Vietnam: The War That Broke the Consensus
Think About It
America spent 20 years in Vietnam, lost 58,000 soldiers, and withdrew without winning. What does that experience reveal about the limits of American power?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. soldiers on patrol in Vietnam, c.1966 — jungle terrain, guerrilla tactics, and an elusive enemy frustrated every conventional military strategy. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Selma march, 1965 — MLK explicitly connected the Civil Rights and anti-Vietnam movements. Dying for democracy abroad while being denied it at home. Library of Congress.
Vietnam was where the Cold War logic of "contain communism everywhere" ran into the reality of fighting a people who had been resisting foreign occupation for centuries, first France, then Japan, then France again, then the U.S. By 1968, the Tet Offensive showed the war was unwinnable. By 1975, South Vietnam had fallen. The political and cultural damage at home outlasted the military defeat.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Escalation
The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) gave LBJ a blank check for war. By 1968, 500,000 U.S. troops were in Vietnam. The Domino Theory said if Vietnam fell, all of Southeast Asia would follow.
›
Step 2
The Unraveling
Tet Offensive (1968): North Vietnam attacked 100 cities simultaneously, shattering public confidence. Pentagon Papers (1971): revealed the government had lied about the war's progress for years.
›
Step 3
The Legacy
Vietnam + Watergate destroyed public trust in government. The "credibility gap" between what officials said and reality became a permanent feature of American political culture.
🔗
Real World Connection
The phrase "boots on the ground" is used carefully today because politicians know the public remembers Vietnam. The war created a specific kind of American skepticism about military intervention that still shapes foreign policy debates, especially about long wars in places with complicated histories of resisting occupation.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Vietnam War failed because... and it changed America by..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Was Vietnam unwinnable from the beginning, or did strategic mistakes and leadership failures cost the U.S. the war?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through why the U.S. got into Vietnam, why public opinion turned, and what the long-term consequences were. Connect it to Watergate: what did both events do to American trust?
