Standard 6.01
The Post-WWII World Order
Think About It
After the most destructive war in history, the world tried to build institutions that would prevent it from ever happening again. Why do you think that's so difficult — and did it work?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Nazi defendants at the Nuremberg Trials, 1945 — for the first time in history, national leaders were prosecuted for crimes against humanity. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Post-WWII Europe and the Iron Curtain — the wartime alliance became the Cold War within two years. The Marshall Plan stabilized the West while the Soviet Union consolidated the East. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Nazi defendants at the Nuremberg Trials, 1945 — for the first time in history, national leaders were prosecuted for crimes against humanity. 'I was following orders' was rejected as a defense. Library of Congress.
The Nuremberg Trials
  • 24 top Nazi leaders prosecuted — 12 sentenced to death, including key military and political leaders
  • Three categories: crimes against peace, war crimes, and crimes against humanity
  • Defense of "following orders" was explicitly rejected — individuals are responsible under international law
  • Established the precedent: heads of state and military commanders can be prosecuted for atrocities
Need a starting point?
"The Nuremberg Trials were historically significant because…"
The United Nations
  • Founded April 1945 — 50 nations at the San Francisco Conference; U.S. joined (unlike the League of Nations)
  • Security Council: 5 permanent members with veto power — U.S., UK, France, USSR, China
  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948): established global standards for human rights
  • More effective than the League because U.S. participated and the structure acknowledged great-power realities
Need a starting point?
"The United Nations was designed differently from the League of Nations because…"
Truman's Foreign Policy
  • Truman Doctrine (1947): U.S. would support free peoples resisting communist takeover — $400 million to Greece and Turkey
  • Marshall Plan (1948): $12 billion to rebuild Western European economies — prevent communism from taking root in poverty
  • Berlin Airlift (1948-49): Soviet blockade of Berlin — U.S. airlifted supplies for 11 months until Soviets backed down
  • NATO (1949): collective defense treaty — first peacetime military alliance in U.S. history
Need a starting point?
"The Truman Doctrine and Marshall Plan were two different tools for fighting communism — one used military aid, the other…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. helped rebuild Western Europe with the Marshall Plan — but did it do so out of generosity, or were American strategic interests driving the decision? Does motivation matter if the outcome was good?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through Nuremberg, the United Nations, and Truman's foreign policy. What do these three things together tell you about what the U.S. was trying to build after WWII — and why?

Standard 6.02
Post-WWII Prosperity and Its Limits
Think About It
The 1950s are often called America's golden age — suburbs, TVs, cars, and rising prosperity. But golden age for whom? And at what cost?

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Levittown, New York — the first mass-produced suburb, built for returning WWII veterans. The American Dream of the 1950s came with an asterisk: most Black veterans could not buy homes here. Library of Congress.
GI Bill & Baby Boom
  • GI Bill (1944): paid for veterans' college, job training, and home loans — 8 million veterans used the education benefit
  • Baby Boom: birth rate spiked 1946-1964 — 76 million babies born in this generation
  • Suburbs exploded: Levittown-style developments offered affordable homes to veterans; Interstate Highway Act (1956) connected them
  • Middle class expanded dramatically — rising wages, home ownership, consumer spending all reached new highs
Need a starting point?
"The GI Bill transformed American society by…"
Television & Youth Culture
  • TV in homes: 9% in 1950 to 90% in 1960 — transformed how Americans consumed news and entertainment
  • Shaped politics: Eisenhower was first president to use TV ads; Army-McCarthy hearings on TV undermined McCarthy
  • Rock and roll emerged: blending Black rhythm and blues with white country — created generational divide
  • Teenagers became a distinct consumer market — movies, music, fashion; "youth culture" was born as a concept
Need a starting point?
"Television transformed American life in the 1950s because…"
Who Was Left Out
  • GI Bill discriminated in practice: Southern banks denied loans to Black veterans; Black veterans excluded from suburban developments
  • African Americans: still subject to Jim Crow in the South; de facto segregation in Northern cities
  • Women: pushed back into domestic roles after wartime work — "The Feminine Mystique" documented this discontent in 1963
  • Beat Generation: Kerouac, Ginsberg, others rejected 1950s conformity and consumer culture entirely
Need a starting point?
"Despite post-war prosperity, African Americans and women were left out because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The 1950s are often remembered as a simpler, better time. Who gets to remember it that way — and whose experience of the 1950s is being left out of that picture?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe post-WWII prosperity — what it looked like and who it served. Then explain: is it fair to call the 1950s a "golden age" for America?

Standard 6.03
The Second Red Scare & McCarthyism
Think About It
Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed communists were secretly infiltrating American government — and for years, people were afraid to challenge him. What does it take for a democracy to allow that kind of accusation to go unchecked?

🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

McCarthyism
  • HUAC (House Un-American Activities Committee): investigated suspected communists in government and entertainment
  • Alger Hiss: State Department official convicted of perjury linked to Soviet espionage — gave McCarthy credibility
  • McCarthy claimed he had a list of 205 communists in the State Department — never produced evidence
  • McCarthyism: making accusations of disloyalty without evidence — destroying careers and silencing dissent
Need a starting point?
"McCarthyism damaged American democracy because…"
Hollywood & Espionage
  • Hollywood Ten: 10 screenwriters and directors refused to testify before HUAC — imprisoned and blacklisted
  • Blacklist: hundreds of entertainers lost careers because of suspected communist sympathies or refusing to name names
  • Rosenbergs: Julius and Ethel Rosenberg executed (1953) for passing atomic secrets to the Soviets
  • Spy anxiety intensified dramatically after the Soviet atomic bomb test in 1949
Need a starting point?
"The Hollywood blacklist showed how McCarthyism operated by…"
Nuclear Fear
  • Soviet atomic bomb test (August 1949): shocked Americans — the U.S. nuclear monopoly was over
  • H-bomb: U.S. developed hydrogen bomb (1952), Soviets matched it (1953) — thousands of times more destructive than Hiroshima
  • Duck-and-cover: school children practiced nuclear attack drills — fear of nuclear war pervaded everyday life
  • MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction): both sides had enough weapons to destroy civilization — made using them suicidal
Need a starting point?
"The nuclear arms race shaped American society by creating…"
Let's Go Deeper
McCarthy was eventually stopped — but only after destroying hundreds of careers. What finally ended his power? And what does the McCarthyism episode reveal about democracy's vulnerabilities?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what McCarthyism was and how it happened. Then think about what it tells us about how fear of a foreign threat can be used to suppress freedom at home.

Standard 6.04
The Korean War
Think About It
The Korean War is sometimes called "the Forgotten War" — yet over 36,000 Americans died in it. Why do you think it's been largely forgotten compared to WWII and Vietnam?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. Marines during the Chosin Reservoir campaign, December 1950 — outnumbered and in brutal winter conditions, Marines fought their way out in one of the war's defining moments. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Korean War, 1950–1953 — front lines seesawed dramatically before stabilizing almost exactly where the war started, at the 38th parallel. The divided peninsula remains today. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. Marines during the Chosin Reservoir campaign, December 1950 — outnumbered 8-to-1 by Chinese forces in brutal winter conditions, Marines fought their way out in a defining battle. Library of Congress.
Causes
  • Korea divided at the 38th parallel after WWII: Soviet-backed communist North, U.S.-backed South
  • June 25, 1950: North Korea invaded South with Soviet-supplied weapons — swept rapidly toward the Pusan Perimeter
  • Truman committed U.S. forces without a congressional declaration of war — called it a "police action"
  • UN authorized force — first major UN military action; 15 nations sent troops
Need a starting point?
"The Korean War began because…"
Course of the War
  • MacArthur's Inchon landing (September 1950): bold amphibious attack cut off North Korean supply lines — brilliant tactical success
  • U.N. forces pushed north to the Chinese border — China had warned against this
  • China entered (October 1950) with 300,000 troops — pushed U.N. forces back below the 38th parallel
  • Truman fired MacArthur (April 1951): MacArthur publicly demanded to attack China — civilian control of the military was at stake
Need a starting point?
"The course of the Korean War changed dramatically when…"
Consequences
  • Armistice signed July 1953 — both sides returned to the 38th parallel — same line where the war started
  • 36,574 Americans killed; 100,000+ wounded; millions of Korean civilian deaths on both sides
  • Divided Korea still exists today — the DMZ is the most heavily fortified border in the world
  • Established the "limited war" precedent — fighting for containment, not unconditional victory
Need a starting point?
"The Korean War established important precedents including…"
Let's Go Deeper
The Korean War ended with Korea still divided — essentially where it started. Was it worth fighting? What did the U.S. actually accomplish?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the Korean War — why it started, what happened, and what it accomplished. Then explain why Truman's firing of MacArthur mattered beyond just the military situation.

Standard 6.05
Cold War Escalation
Think About It
Two superpowers with enough nuclear weapons to end civilization — and they never directly fought each other. How did both sides manage to stay just below the line of actual war for four decades?

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Sputnik 1 — the Soviet satellite launched October 4, 1957, shocked the U.S. and launched the Space Race. If the Soviets could orbit a satellite, they could deliver a nuclear warhead anywhere on Earth. Library of Congress / NASA.
Arms Race & Berlin
  • Both superpowers built thousands of nuclear warheads — MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction) made using them suicidal
  • CIA covert operations: overthrew governments in Iran (1953) and Guatemala (1954) — covert Cold War front
  • Berlin Wall (1961): East Germany built it overnight — divided the city; became the defining symbol of the Iron Curtain
  • Eisenhower's "New Look": relied on nuclear deterrence and CIA rather than expensive conventional forces
Need a starting point?
"The Cold War heated up in the late 1950s because…"
Sputnik & the Space Race
  • Sputnik (October 1957): Soviet satellite shocked the U.S. — Americans had assumed technological superiority
  • If Soviets could orbit a satellite, they could deliver a nuclear warhead anywhere on Earth
  • NASA created 1958: Congress passed National Aeronautics and Space Act — space became a Cold War arena
  • National Defense Education Act (1958): $1 billion for science, math, and language education — Sputnik changed U.S. schools
Need a starting point?
"Sputnik changed America because it proved…"
Cuba & the Missile Crisis
  • Cuban Revolution (1959): Castro overthrew a U.S.-backed dictator — aligned Cuba with the Soviet Union
  • Bay of Pigs (1961): Kennedy's CIA-trained Cuban exiles failed to overthrow Castro — an embarrassing failure
  • Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962): U.S. discovered Soviet nuclear missiles in Cuba — 13 days on the edge of nuclear war
  • Resolution: Soviets removed missiles; U.S. quietly agreed not to invade Cuba and to remove missiles from Turkey
Need a starting point?
"The Cuban Missile Crisis was the closest the Cold War came to actual nuclear war because…"
Let's Go Deeper
During the Cuban Missile Crisis, both Kennedy and Khrushchev made concessions to avoid nuclear war. What does that tell you about how nuclear weapons actually functioned as tools of foreign policy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through Cold War escalation from the late 1950s into the 1960s. Focus on one moment — Sputnik, Berlin, or the Cuban Missile Crisis — and explain why it mattered so much.

The Vietnam War and Its Impact
Think About It
America spent 20 years in Vietnam, lost 58,000 soldiers, and ultimately withdrew without winning. What does that experience tell you about the limits of American power?

Map

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. soldiers on patrol in Vietnam, c.1966 — the war's jungle terrain and guerrilla tactics frustrated conventional military strategy. 58,000 Americans and millions of Vietnamese died. Library of Congress.
Causes & Escalation
  • Vietnam divided at 17th parallel (1954) — communist North, U.S.-backed South
  • Domino Theory: if South Vietnam fell, surrounding nations would follow — drove U.S. involvement
  • Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964): Congress gave LBJ broad authority to escalate after disputed attack on U.S. ships
  • By 1968: 500,000 U.S. troops in Vietnam — Tet Offensive showed the war was far from won
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. became deeply involved in Vietnam because of the Cold War policy of…"
Changing Public Opinion
  • Tet Offensive (1968): North Vietnamese attacked 100 cities — shattered official claims of progress
  • Anti-war movement: college campuses, civil rights leaders, and returning veterans all organized against the war
  • Kent State (1970): National Guard killed 4 students at an anti-war protest — shocked the nation
  • Pentagon Papers (1971): leaked documents revealed the government had repeatedly lied about the war's progress
Need a starting point?
"American public opinion turned against the Vietnam War because…"
Watergate & Legacy
  • Watergate: Nixon's campaign operatives broke into Democratic HQ (1972) — Nixon covered it up
  • Congressional investigation revealed Nixon had recorded Oval Office conversations proving his involvement
  • Nixon resigned August 9, 1974 — the first and only presidential resignation in U.S. history
  • Legacy: Vietnam and Watergate together shattered American public trust in government — the "credibility gap"
Need a starting point?
"The Watergate scandal mattered because it demonstrated that…"
Let's Go Deeper
Some argue Vietnam was unwinnable from the beginning. Others say strategic mistakes and leadership failures cost the U.S. the war. Which argument do you find more convincing?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the Vietnam War — why the U.S. got involved, how public opinion changed, and what the long-term consequences were. Then connect it to Watergate: what did both events do to American trust in government?
