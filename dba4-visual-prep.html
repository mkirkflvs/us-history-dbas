Standard 4.01
Fear, Power, and the First Red Scare
Think About It
After winning a world war for democracy, America immediately started arresting people for their political opinions. Why does winning sometimes make countries more afraid, not less?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A 1919 cartoon depicting Bolshevism as a spreading threat — the visual language of fear, used to justify suspending civil liberties. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Two American soldiers across two wars — WWI veterans came home to a government that immediately turned its security apparatus against labor organizers and immigrants. Library of Congress.
The Russian Revolution of 1917 rattled the Western world. When four million American workers went on strike in 1919, politicians and newspapers immediately blamed Bolshevik agitators. Attorney General Palmer launched mass arrests without evidence. It was the first big test of whether America would sacrifice civil liberties in the name of security.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Fear
Communist revolution in Russia, labor strikes at home, and mail bombs sent to politicians created a climate of panic. Any left-wing politics became "un-American."
›
Step 2
The Crackdown
The Palmer Raids arrested 10,000 people without warrants. Many were deported. The Sacco and Vanzetti case executed two Italian anarchists, most historians now believe for their politics, not their crime.
›
Step 3
The Lesson
Fear is a political tool. The Red Scare destroyed labor organizing, silenced dissent, and targeted immigrants, exactly the groups powerful people wanted to control.
🔗
Real World Connection
The pattern of "foreign influence" being used to discredit domestic movements appears throughout American history. Red Scare in 1919, McCarthyism in the 1950s, and echoes today. Ask yourself who benefits when a movement gets labeled as foreign-controlled.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Red Scare was really about... not just about..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Palmer said he was protecting democracy from communism. But his methods, mass arrests without warrants, deportations without trials, seem pretty undemocratic. How do you evaluate that?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain the Red Scare and what it reveals about how fear gets weaponized in politics.

Standard 4.02
The Roaring Twenties: Real Prosperity, Hidden Cracks
Think About It
The 1920s were the best of times and the worst of times, depending on who you were. What made the prosperity feel so real and why was it so fragile?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Ford's assembly line — mass production transformed American consumer culture. The Model T dropped from $850 in 1909 to $300 in 1925. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Breadline under the Brooklyn Bridge — within a decade of the 1920s boom, this is what the same streets looked like. Library of Congress.
For many Americans, the 1920s genuinely were prosperous. Cars, radios, movies, and consumer credit transformed daily life. Mass production made things cheap. But the boom was built on borrowed money, stock speculation, and the complete exclusion of farmers and Black Americans, and that foundation was rotting the whole time.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Boom
GNP grew 40%. Ford's assembly line made cars affordable. "Buy now, pay later" credit opened up consumption. By 1929, one in five Americans owned a car.
›
Step 2
The Cracks
Farmers never shared in the prosperity, crop prices had been collapsing since WWI ended. Stock speculation was rampant. Banks were lending money that didn't really exist.
›
Step 3
The Setup for Disaster
When stock prices dropped in October 1929, the whole credit-fueled system unraveled at once. Banks failed, businesses closed, 15 million lost their jobs in three years.
🔗
Real World Connection
Sound familiar? The 2008 financial crisis followed almost the same pattern, a credit bubble, speculative investment, growing inequality, and then a crash that hit ordinary people hardest while the people who caused it mostly survived. History doesn't exactly repeat, but it rhymes.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The 1920s economy looked strong but was actually fragile because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
In 1929, most economists didn't see the crash coming. What warning signs existed, and why did smart people miss them?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe the 1920s economy: what was real, what was a mirage, and who was left out of the prosperity entirely.

Standard 4.03
The Culture Wars of the 1920s
Think About It
The 1920s produced flappers, jazz, and the Harlem Renaissance, and also the KKK's largest membership ever. How does liberation and backlash happen in the same decade?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
James Van Der Zee portrait, Harlem, 1920s — Van Der Zee documented the Renaissance's vitality and pride deliberately and beautifully. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
KKK march, Pennsylvania Avenue, 1926 — at its peak, 4 million members. The same decade that produced the Harlem Renaissance produced this. Library of Congress.
The 1920s were a decade of genuine cultural revolution and vicious reaction against it. Women got the vote, jazz spread from Black communities into mainstream culture, and the Harlem Renaissance created an explosion of Black art, literature, and pride. At the same time, the KKK had 4 million members, Prohibition was a national joke, and fundamentalist Christianity declared war on modern science.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Revolution
Flappers rejected Victorian gender norms. Jazz, a Black art form, crossed racial lines and scandalized conservatives. Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, and Duke Ellington created a new American culture.
›
Step 2
The Backlash
Four million KKK members in 1924. The Scopes Trial put evolution on trial. The KKK in the 1920s wasn't just Southern, it was strong in Indiana, Oregon, and Colorado.
›
Step 3
The Contradiction
The same decade that celebrated liberation and creativity also produced some of the most organized racial and religious bigotry in American history. Both were real. Both were American.
🔗
Real World Connection
Look at how cultural change and cultural backlash interact today. When one group gains visibility and power, there's almost always a reaction from people who feel threatened by that change. The 1920s shows this has been a repeating pattern for a long time.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The 1920s were contradictory because at the same time as... there was also..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The Harlem Renaissance created some of the greatest art and literature in American history, while the KKK was at its peak. How do you make sense of that about American culture?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Pick two forces from the 1920s that seem contradictory, liberation and backlash, prosperity and exclusion, and explain what they together reveal about American society.

Standard 4.04
Nativism: The Fear of the "Other"
Think About It
What happens when "American" gets defined as white, Protestant, and native-born, and everyone else gets told to leave or conform?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Detained immigrants at Ellis Island — the Immigration Act of 1924 was designed to stop exactly these people from ever arriving. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Uncle Sam expanding globally — the same decade America was restricting immigration at home, it was building empire abroad. Library of Congress.
The 1920s saw a wave of nativist sentiment that produced some of the most restrictive immigration laws in U.S. history and revived the KKK as a mainstream political organization. The fear wasn't just of communism or crime, it was of demographic and cultural change. And people fought back, from the NAACP to Marcus Garvey's Black nationalist movement.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Laws
The Immigration Act of 1924 set quotas that essentially banned Southern and Eastern European immigrants and completely blocked most Asian immigration. It was openly based on racial hierarchy.
›
Step 2
The Violence
The 1920s KKK had 4 million members at its peak and wasn't just about race, it targeted Catholics, Jews, and immigrants too. It operated openly, marching down Pennsylvania Avenue.
›
Step 3
The Resistance
The NAACP published data on lynching. Marcus Garvey built the largest Black mass movement in American history. Jewish and Catholic communities organized politically. Resistance was real.
🔗
Real World Connection
The arguments used to restrict immigration in 1924, they're changing our culture, they don't assimilate, they're taking our jobs, are nearly word-for-word the arguments used in immigration debates today. What does that tell you?
🤔 Need a starting point?
"Nativism in the 1920s was driven by... and it resulted in..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The KKK of the 1920s was at its strongest in the Midwest and the Pacific Northwest, not the South. What does that tell you about the geography of racism in America?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what nativism meant in the 1920s, who it targeted, and how different groups pushed back against it.

Standard 4.05
The Great Depression: When Everything Collapsed
Think About It
In 1929, the economy was booming. By 1933, 1 in 4 Americans was unemployed. How does an entire economic system collapse in four years?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Dorothea Lange, FSA photograph, 1930s — Lange's images gave the Depression a human face and built political support for relief. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Hooverville, Ohio, 1936 — cardboard shack towns appeared in every American city as millions lost their homes. Library of Congress.
The Great Depression was the worst economic catastrophe in American history. It started with the stock market crash of 1929, but the deeper causes were structural: overproduction, credit speculation, bank failures, and a global trade collapse. President Hoover believed government shouldn't intervene directly, by the time he changed his mind, millions were homeless and starving.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Collapse
Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929) wiped out $14 billion in one day. Banks called in loans. Businesses closed. 9,000 banks failed in three years, taking people's savings with them.
›
Step 2
The Human Cost
Hoovervilles, cardboard shack towns, appeared in every city. The Dust Bowl forced 3.5 million people off Great Plains farms. Black and immigrant communities hit hardest, unemployment near 50%.
›
Step 3
The Wrong Response
Hoover raised tariffs, cutting off global trade. He gave loans to banks but not to people. He sent the Army to evict WWI veterans demanding their bonus in Washington. It made everything worse.
🔗
Real World Connection
During COVID-19, the government sent stimulus checks directly to Americans within weeks. During the Depression, Hoover refused to do anything like that for three years. The difference isn't just about technology, it's about what government thinks it's responsible for.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Great Depression was caused by... and it showed that..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Hoover wasn't heartless, he genuinely believed government aid would destroy American character and initiative. Why did that philosophy fail so completely during the Depression?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the causes of the Great Depression and what it felt like to live through it. Then explain why Hoover's approach made it worse.

Standard 4.06
FDR's New Deal: Did It Work?
Think About It
FDR promised "a new deal for the American people." What does it actually mean for the government to reshape the economy, and was it worth the cost?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
TVA dam construction, 1930s — the New Deal put millions to work and brought electricity to Appalachia for the first time. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
FDR Memorial breadline sculpture — a permanent reminder of what the New Deal was created to address. Library of Congress.
FDR's New Deal transformed the relationship between the federal government and American citizens. Programs like Social Security, the FDIC, and the Wagner Act created a safety net that still exists today. Critics called it socialism. Supporters called it saving capitalism from itself. The truth is somewhere in between, and the Depression didn't actually end until WWII.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Programs
CCC put young men to work in forests. FDIC insured bank deposits. TVA brought electricity to rural Appalachia. WPA employed 8 million people in public works. Social Security created a retirement safety net.
›
Step 2
The Opposition
The Supreme Court struck down key New Deal laws. Business leaders hated union protections. FDR's court-packing plan (adding 6 justices) was rejected even by his own party as a power grab.
›
Step 3
The Legacy
The New Deal didn't end the Depression. WWII did. But it permanently changed what Americans expect from government: a floor below which citizens shouldn't fall. Social Security alone still reaches 70 million people.
🔗
Real World Connection
When you hear debates about minimum wage, Medicare, or unemployment benefits, you're hearing echoes of the New Deal debate. The fundamental question, how much should government protect people from economic forces?, has never been resolved.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The New Deal changed America by... but it also had limits because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
FDR's New Deal created the modern welfare state. Critics said it destroyed American self-reliance. Supporters said it saved capitalism. Who's right, or is it more complicated than either position?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the major New Deal programs and explain their lasting impact. Then address: did the New Deal actually fix the Depression?
