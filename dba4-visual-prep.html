Standard 4.01
The Red Scare & International Peace Efforts
Think About It
The war had just ended — but Americans were terrified of a new threat: communism. What do you think it felt like to live in a country where your political beliefs could land you in prison or get you deported?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A 1919 political cartoon depicting Bolshevism as a spreading threat — the Red Scare gripped American society in the years after WWI. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Post-WWI Europe — communist revolutions swept Russia and threatened Germany and Hungary, convincing many Americans that Bolshevism was spreading westward. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A 1919 Red Scare cartoon — the Palmer Raids arrested 10,000 people without warrants. Fear of foreign-born 'agitators' drove the raids, showing how quickly civil liberties collapse under political panic. Library of Congress.
What Was the Red Scare
  • Russian Revolution (1917): Bolsheviks took power — world's first communist state alarmed Western governments
  • Labor strikes (1919): 4 million workers struck in a single year — many Americans blamed "Bolshevik agitators"
  • Mail bombs sent to politicians and businessmen — anarchist attacks increased fear
  • Palmer Raids (1919-1920): Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer arrested 10,000 suspected radicals without warrants
Need a starting point?
"The Red Scare began because Americans feared…"
Government Response
  • Palmer Raids: mass arrests of immigrants and suspected communists — most held without evidence
  • J. Edgar Hoover built his career targeting suspected radicals — became FBI director in 1924
  • Sacco and Vanzetti: Italian anarchists executed for murder (1927) — many believed convicted for their politics
  • States passed criminal syndicalism laws — making it illegal to advocate for overthrowing the government
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. government responded to the Red Scare by…"
International Peace Efforts
  • Four-Power Treaty (1921): U.S., Britain, France, Japan agreed to respect each other's Pacific territories
  • Washington Naval Conference (1921-22): limited the size of major navies — first arms control agreement
  • Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928): 62 nations renounced war as a tool of foreign policy — no enforcement mechanism
  • These agreements showed the U.S. wanted peace but rejected League of Nations membership
Need a starting point?
"Post-WWI peace efforts were significant but limited because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The Red Scare led to mass arrests and deportations without evidence. How does that compare to other moments in U.S. history when fear led the government to violate civil liberties?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what the Red Scare was and why it happened when it did. Then explain what the government's response tells us about how fear shapes policy.

Standard 4.02
Economic Trends of the 1920s
Think About It
The 1920s were roaring for some Americans — new cars, radios, movies, and credit to buy it all. But not everyone was celebrating. Who got left out of the prosperity?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Ford's Model T assembly line — Henry Ford's moving assembly line made cars affordable for the middle class and transformed American consumer culture. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The 1920s consumer economy — by 1929 one in five Americans owned a car. Mass production, consumer credit, and advertising created a new culture of spending. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Ford's Model T assembly line, c.1913 — Henry Ford's moving assembly line made cars affordable for the middle class and transformed American consumer culture. Library of Congress.
The Boom & Consumer Culture
  • GNP grew 40% during the 1920s — real prosperity for the middle class
  • Consumer credit ("buy now, pay later") let Americans buy cars, radios, and appliances
  • Advertising industry grew massively — manufactured desire for new consumer goods
  • Stock market speculation: millions invested in stocks on margin — borrowed money to buy shares
Need a starting point?
"The economic boom of the 1920s was driven by…"
Mass Production
  • Henry Ford's moving assembly line made the Model T affordable — price dropped from $850 (1909) to $300 (1925)
  • Mass production spread to all consumer goods — refrigerators, washing machines, radios
  • Electrification: by 1929, two-thirds of American homes had electricity — transformed daily life
  • New industries emerged as major economic forces: movies, radio, and aviation
Need a starting point?
"Mass production changed American life because it made…"
Who Was Left Behind
  • Farmers: crop prices collapsed after WWI — rural poverty worsened throughout the 1920s
  • Farm debt: many borrowed heavily during WWI boom, couldn't repay when prices fell
  • Factory workers: wages rose but not as fast as corporate profits — inequality grew
  • African Americans: still locked out of economic gains by segregation and discrimination
Need a starting point?
"Despite the boom, farmers and many workers were left behind because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The 1920s economy looked strong — but was it built on solid foundations? What warning signs existed before the crash of 1929?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe the 1920s economy and explain who it really worked for. Then identify what warning signs, in retrospect, suggest the good times couldn't last.

Standard 4.03
Cultural Change in the 1920s
Think About It
The 1920s produced the flapper, jazz, the Harlem Renaissance — and also the KKK's largest membership in history. What does it say about a decade when liberation and backlash exist side by side?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A portrait by photographer James Van Der Zee capturing Harlem's vitality during the Harlem Renaissance — a flowering of Black art, music, and literature. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Great Migration, 1910–1930 — over one million Black Americans moved north to escape Jim Crow and find industrial jobs, creating communities that produced the Harlem Renaissance. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
James Van Der Zee portrait, Harlem, 1920s — Van Der Zee documented the Harlem Renaissance's flowering of Black culture, art, and middle-class life. Library of Congress.
Women & Prohibition
  • 19th Amendment (1920): women won the right to vote after a 70-year struggle
  • Flappers: young women rejected Victorian norms — shorter skirts, jazz clubs, driving cars
  • Prohibition (18th Amendment, 1920): banned manufacture and sale of alcohol — led to speakeasies and organized crime
  • Prohibition failed to stop drinking — only drove it underground and created criminal empires (Al Capone)
Need a starting point?
"The 1920s represented a turning point for women because…"
The Harlem Renaissance
  • Great Migration brought Black Americans north — Harlem became a cultural center
  • Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Claude McKay: literary explosion of Black voices and experiences
  • Jazz: Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington — created a new American art form rooted in Black tradition
  • Marcus Garvey: Black nationalism and pride — "Back to Africa" movement; self-determination
Need a starting point?
"The Harlem Renaissance mattered because it…"
Fundamentalist Pushback
  • Fundamentalism: religious movement insisting on the literal truth of the Bible — saw modern culture as a threat
  • Scopes Trial (1925): Tennessee teacher put on trial for teaching evolution — fundamentalism vs. modern science in public
  • KKK revived: 4 million members at peak — targeted Black Americans, Catholics, Jews, and immigrants
  • Celebrity preachers like Billy Sunday fought against modern secular culture from pulpits across the country
Need a starting point?
"Fundamentalists pushed back against the cultural changes of the 1920s because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The 1920s are often called a decade of freedom and liberation — but for whom? How does the Harlem Renaissance sit alongside the KKK revival in the same decade?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
The 1920s were full of contradictions — liberation and backlash at the same time. Pick two examples that show this tension and explain what they reveal about American society.

Standard 4.04
Nativism, Civil Rights & the 1920s
Think About It
After WWI, the U.S. turned inward — and uglier. Immigrants were restricted, Black Americans were terrorized, and "Americanism" was used to exclude people. What happens when fear and nationalism intersect?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
KKK march down Pennsylvania Avenue, Washington D.C., 1926 — at its 1920s peak the Klan had four million members and operated openly in politics. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
KKK membership and nativist activity, 1920s — the Klan was strongest not just in the South but across the Midwest. At its peak, four million members operated openly in politics. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
KKK march down Pennsylvania Avenue, Washington D.C., 1926 — the Klan operated openly in the nation's capital. At its peak it had four million members and significant political influence. Library of Congress.
Rise of Nativism
  • Nativism: belief that native-born (white, Protestant) Americans are superior to immigrants and minorities
  • Emergency Quota Act (1921): immigration quotas based on the 1910 census — disadvantaged newer immigrant groups
  • Immigration Act of 1924: set strict national-origin quotas favoring Northern Europeans; virtually banned Asian immigration
  • Total immigration reduced to 165,000 per year — a dramatic restriction from pre-war levels
Need a starting point?
"Nativism rose in the 1920s because Americans feared…"
How People Fought Against Civil Rights
  • KKK: 4 million members at peak — murdered, terrorized, and intimidated Black Americans, Catholics, Jews
  • Sundown towns: thousands of communities where Black people were forbidden to remain after dark
  • Restrictive covenants: legal contracts preventing home sales to Black buyers — locked them out of white neighborhoods
  • Lynching continued: hundreds of racial killings; federal anti-lynching legislation repeatedly blocked in Congress
Need a starting point?
"In the 1920s, racial discrimination and terror continued because…"
How People Fought For Civil Rights
  • NAACP: legal challenges to segregation; published data on lynching to build public pressure for federal action
  • Marcus Garvey's UNIA: Black economic self-sufficiency and racial pride — largest Black movement of the era
  • Anti-Defamation League (1913): fought antisemitism — grew in response to KKK and nativist attacks
  • Ida B. Wells: documented lynching throughout her life; demanded federal anti-lynching legislation
Need a starting point?
"Groups fighting for civil rights in the 1920s used strategies like…"
Let's Go Deeper
Nativism and anti-Black violence both intensified in the 1920s alongside prosperity and "freedom." What does that tell you about whose freedom the 1920s actually celebrated?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about who was included and who was excluded from 1920s prosperity and freedom. Walk through specific examples of how nativism and racial terror worked — and how people pushed back.

Standard 4.05
Causes and Impact of the Great Depression
Think About It
In 1929 the U.S. economy was booming. By 1933, one in four Americans was unemployed. How does an economy collapse that fast — and what do people do when the safety net disappears entirely?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A family photographed during the Great Depression — Dorothea Lange's images for the Farm Security Administration documented the human cost of the economic collapse. Library of Congress / FSA.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. unemployment rates, 1933 — at the Depression's worst, 24.9% of Americans were out of work. Every region was hit, but manufacturing centers and farming communities suffered most. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Dorothea Lange, Farm Security Administration photograph, 1930s — Lange's images gave the Great Depression a human face and built political support for New Deal relief. Library of Congress.
What Caused It
  • Stock market speculation: millions borrowed money to buy stocks — when prices fell, they couldn't repay
  • Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929): market crashed — $14 billion lost in a single day
  • Bank failures: panic withdrawals caused banks to collapse — 9,000 banks failed between 1930 and 1933
  • Overproduction: factories and farms produced more than consumers could buy — prices fell, layoffs followed
Need a starting point?
"The Great Depression was caused by a combination of factors including…"
How People Were Affected
  • Unemployment reached 24.9% by 1933 — 15 million Americans out of work
  • Hoovervilles: shantytowns of cardboard and scrap where homeless families lived — named to mock Hoover
  • Dust Bowl: drought and over-farming destroyed Great Plains topsoil — 3.5 million people fled westward
  • African Americans and minorities hit hardest — "last hired, first fired" — unemployment reached 50% in some communities
Need a starting point?
"Ordinary Americans were affected by the Great Depression in ways like…"
Hoover's Response
  • Hoover believed government should not directly aid individuals — voluntary business cooperation instead
  • Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930): raised tariffs to protect industry — other countries retaliated; global trade collapsed
  • Reconstruction Finance Corporation: lent money to banks and railroads — critics said it helped the rich, not workers
  • Bonus Army (1932): WWI veterans marched on Washington — Hoover ordered military to remove them by force
Need a starting point?
"Hoover's response to the Great Depression failed because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Herbert Hoover wasn't heartless — he believed he was following sound economic principles. Why do you think his approach failed so completely?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the causes of the Great Depression and what it felt like to live through it. Then explain why Hoover's approach didn't work — and what the country needed instead.

Standard 4.06
The New Deal
Think About It
Roosevelt came to power promising a "New Deal for the American people." What would it actually take for the government to step in and reshape the economy — and who would fight back against that?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Workers building a Tennessee Valley Authority dam — New Deal public works projects employed millions and transformed American infrastructure. Library of Congress / FSA.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
New Deal programs across the U.S. — the CCC, WPA, TVA, and others reached into every region, putting millions to work and transforming American infrastructure. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
TVA dam construction, 1930s — New Deal public works employed millions and transformed American infrastructure. The TVA brought electricity to rural Appalachia for the first time. Library of Congress.
Programs & Relief
  • First New Deal (1933): emergency relief — CCC put young men to work; FDIC insured bank deposits; AAA paid farmers to reduce surplus production
  • Second New Deal (1935): long-term reform — Social Security Act, Wagner Act protected unions, WPA employed 8 million in public works
  • TVA: built dams across the Tennessee Valley — electricity to rural areas, flood control, jobs
  • FDIC: insured deposits up to $2,500 — restored public confidence in banks and stopped the cycle of panics
Need a starting point?
"The New Deal tried to address the Depression through programs like…"
Opposition
  • Conservatives: criticized as socialist overreach — government was accumulating too much power
  • Business community: hated the pro-union Wagner Act and new regulations on industry
  • Supreme Court: struck down AAA and NIRA as unconstitutional — "nine old men"
  • FDR's court-packing plan (1937): proposed adding 6 new justices — Congress rejected it; widely seen as a power grab
Need a starting point?
"Critics opposed the New Deal because they believed…"
Long-Term Legacy
  • Social Security (1935): created a permanent safety net for elderly and unemployed Americans — still exists today
  • Federal government permanently expanded its role — accepted responsibility for citizens' basic economic welfare
  • Labor unions gained legal protection and grew dramatically — reshaped the balance of power in workplaces
  • National debt grew — established the precedent of deficit spending during economic crises
Need a starting point?
"The most lasting effect of the New Deal was…"
Let's Go Deeper
Did the New Deal actually end the Great Depression — or did it just manage it? What finally ended the economic crisis?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the major programs of the New Deal and explain what they were trying to do. Then think about what FDR's approach actually changed permanently about the relationship between the government and its citizens.
