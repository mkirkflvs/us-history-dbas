Standard 2.01
Causes of the Second Industrial Revolution
Think About It
The first Industrial Revolution had already happened — so what caused a second one? What changed in the late 1800s that made railroads, factories, and cities explode again?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
"Breaker boys" sorting coal at a Pennsylvania mine, 1911 — child labor powered industrial growth. Lewis Hine / Library of Congress, no known restrictions.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Lloyd's Railroad, Telegraph & Express Map of the United States, 1867 — railroads were the circulatory system of the industrial economy. Library of Congress, Geography and Map Division.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'The Tournament of Today — Labor vs. Monopoly,' Puck 1883 — a worker on a starving horse labeled 'Poverty' faces a capitalist riding 'Monopoly.' Jay Gould, Vanderbilt, and other robber barons watch from reserved seats. Library of Congress.
Transportation & Communication
  • Railroads expanded from 35,000 miles (1865) to 200,000+ miles (1900) — created national markets
  • Transcontinental Railroad completed 1869 — connected East to West Coast
  • Telegraph allowed near-instant communication across the country
  • Steel replaced iron rails — stronger, cheaper, and lasting longer
Need a starting point?
"Transportation drove industrialization because…"
Entrepreneurs & Technology
  • John D. Rockefeller (Standard Oil) and Andrew Carnegie (steel) built industrial empires
  • Vertical integration: Carnegie controlled every step from raw material to finished product
  • Horizontal integration: Rockefeller bought out competitors to control the oil industry
  • Patent system rewarded inventors and encouraged technological innovation
Need a starting point?
"Entrepreneurs like Carnegie and Rockefeller changed American industry by…"
Labor & Government
  • Mass immigration provided cheap labor for factories, mines, and construction
  • Laissez-faire government policy — little regulation of business or working conditions
  • Land grants and subsidies encouraged railroad construction
  • Abundant natural resources: Pennsylvania coal, Great Lakes iron ore, Western timber
Need a starting point?
"The government's role in the Second Industrial Revolution was…"
Let's Go Deeper
Was the Second Industrial Revolution primarily driven by technology, by the entrepreneurs who controlled it, or by the workers who powered it? Which factor matters most?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through what caused the Second Industrial Revolution. Which factor — transportation, entrepreneurs, or labor — feels most important to you, and how would you explain that?

Standard 2.01
Effects of Industrialization
Think About It
Industrialization made America incredibly wealthy — but not everyone benefited equally. How do you think where you lived shaped how industrialization affected your life?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Pittsburgh steel mills at full production, c.1900 — the Midwest became the industrial heart of America. Detroit Publishing Co. / Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. population and industrial centers, late 19th century — industrialization concentrated wealth in the Northeast and Midwest while the South lagged behind. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'Breaker Boys,' Pennsylvania coal mine, 1911 — children as young as 8 sorted coal for pennies a day. Lewis Hine's photographs for the National Child Labor Committee helped build public pressure for reform. Library of Congress.
Northeast
  • Already industrialized — textiles and manufacturing continued to grow in Massachusetts and New York
  • Cities like New York, Boston, and Philadelphia became major financial and commercial hubs
  • Immigration surged — tenements, overcrowding, and urban poverty grew alongside wealth
  • Women and children worked long hours in textile mills and garment factories
Need a starting point?
"In the Northeast, industrialization created both wealth and hardship because…"
Midwest & West
  • Chicago became the hub of meatpacking, steel, and railroads — "city of big shoulders"
  • Carnegie's steel production centered in Pittsburgh — enormous output and enormous pollution
  • Great Plains became the breadbasket — mechanized farming replaced manual labor
  • Western mining, logging, and ranching boomed but left environmental and human scars
Need a starting point?
"The Midwest and West were transformed by industrialization when…"
South
  • South lagged behind — still rebuilding after Civil War devastation
  • Cotton remained king but prices dropped — sharecropping kept poverty entrenched
  • Some industrialization in textile mills and tobacco processing — the "New South" vision
  • African Americans were excluded from most industrial jobs and labor unions entirely
Need a starting point?
"Industrialization affected the South differently because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Industrialization created enormous national wealth — but was that wealth shared equally across regions and groups? Who benefited most and who was left behind?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe who actually benefited from industrialization. Did it help everyone or just certain regions and groups? Talk through your thinking with specific examples.

Standard 2.04
The Immigrant Experience
Think About It
Between 1880 and 1920, about 20 million immigrants arrived in the United States. What do you think they were hoping for — and what do you think they actually found?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Immigrants arriving in New York Harbor, c.1902 — millions made this crossing hoping for a better life. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Immigration settlement patterns, 1880–1920 — new arrivals clustered in ethnic neighborhoods in industrial cities, taking the lowest-paid factory and mining jobs. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Immigrants arriving in New York Harbor, c.1902 — millions crossed the Atlantic hoping for a better life. Most found hard work, low wages, and discrimination. Library of Congress.
What Life Was Like
  • Ellis Island: processed 12 million immigrants 1892–1954 — medical and legal inspection on arrival
  • Most settled in ethnic neighborhoods — Little Italy, Chinatown, Jewish Lower East Side
  • Tenement housing: overcrowded, dark, poor sanitation, disease — but all many could afford
  • Took the hardest, most dangerous, lowest-paid industrial jobs — mines, mills, meatpacking
Need a starting point?
"Life for immigrants in industrial America was difficult because…"
How U.S. Citizens Responded
  • Nativism: belief that native-born (white Protestant) Americans were superior to immigrants
  • Fear that immigrants were taking jobs, lowering wages, and changing American culture
  • Social Darwinism used to argue certain groups were biologically inferior
  • Ethnic stereotypes and discrimination in housing, employment, and daily life
Need a starting point?
"Many Americans responded to immigration with hostility because…"
Immigration Laws
  • Chinese Exclusion Act (1882): first law to ban immigration by nationality — prohibited Chinese laborers
  • Gentlemen's Agreement (1907): U.S.-Japan deal limiting Japanese immigration
  • Immigration Act of 1917: literacy test required; barred most Asian immigrants
  • Emergency Quota Act (1921) and Immigration Act of 1924: set strict national-origin quotas favoring Northern Europeans
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. government responded to immigration by passing laws that…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. was built by immigrants — yet in this era, Americans increasingly tried to restrict immigration. How do you make sense of that contradiction?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the immigrant experience around 1900. What did people find when they arrived? Then think about how the laws of this era tried to shape who got to come at all.

Standard 2.06
The Labor Movement
Think About It
Workers in this era could be fired for any reason, had no set working hours, and had no safety protections. What do you think it would take to change that — and who would push back?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Workers organizing for better conditions in the early 1900s — labor activism was dangerous and bitterly contested. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Major U.S. labor strikes, late 19th century — workers organized against dangerous conditions, 12-hour days, and poverty wages. Every major confrontation ended with federal or state troops siding with the owners. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Workers organizing in the early 1900s — labor activism was dangerous and bitterly contested. Strikers faced Pinkerton agents, court injunctions, and federal troops. Library of Congress.
Why Unions Were Needed
  • 12–16 hour workdays were common, 7 days a week in many industries
  • No minimum wage, no overtime pay, no compensation for workplace injuries or death
  • Child labor widespread — children as young as 5 worked in coal mines and textile mills
  • Workers could be fired immediately for any reason — no job security whatsoever
Need a starting point?
"Workers needed unions because working conditions in this era included…"
Strikes & Opposition
  • Knights of Labor (1869): first major national union — skilled and unskilled workers welcome
  • American Federation of Labor (AFL, 1886): Samuel Gompers focused on skilled workers and concrete gains
  • Homestead Strike (1892): Carnegie Steel hired Pinkerton agents to attack striking workers; government sent troops
  • Pullman Strike (1894): 250,000 railroad workers struck — government obtained injunction to break it; Eugene Debs jailed
Need a starting point?
"One of the most important labor conflicts of this era was…because…"
Accomplishments & Limits
  • Won 8-hour workday in some industries — a major concrete gain for workers
  • Raised public awareness of dangerous working conditions through strikes and press coverage
  • Child labor laws passed in some states — federal law not until 1938
  • Women and African Americans largely excluded from major unions — left more vulnerable
Need a starting point?
"The labor movement succeeded in some areas but remained limited because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The government consistently sided with business owners over workers — using courts, troops, and injunctions to break strikes. What does that tell you about who held power in the Gilded Age?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through why workers organized and what they were up against. Pick one strike and explain what it revealed about the balance of power between workers and owners.

Standard 2.05
Political Machines and Reform
Think About It
Political machines were corrupt — but they also provided real services to poor immigrants who had nowhere else to turn. Does that complicate your judgment of them?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thomas Nast's cartoon attacking Boss Tweed and Tammany Hall corruption, 1871 — Nast's cartoons helped bring down the Tweed Ring. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American urban growth, late 19th century — rapidly expanding cities with millions of new immigrants created the conditions for political machine politics. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thomas Nast, Tammany Hall cartoon, Harper's Weekly 1871 — Nast's relentless attacks helped bring down Boss Tweed. Tweed reportedly feared the cartoons more than newspaper articles. Library of Congress.
What Political Machines Were
  • Political machines: organized systems of political control that traded favors for votes
  • Tammany Hall: the Democratic Party machine that controlled New York City politics for decades
  • Boss Tweed: leader of Tammany Hall — stole an estimated $30–200 million from New York City
  • Machines operated in almost every major American city — Chicago, Boston, Philadelphia, Kansas City
Need a starting point?
"Political machines arose because in rapidly growing cities with millions of immigrants…"
How They Were Corrupt
  • Padded city contracts — machine insiders got government business and kicked back a percentage
  • Bought votes: paid cash or gave jobs in exchange for loyal voting
  • Controlled police and courts — charges against machine allies were dropped
  • Used city funds to enrich machine members and their families and friends
Need a starting point?
"Political machines were corrupt because they…"
How Citizens Fought Back
  • Muckrakers like Lincoln Steffens exposed machine corruption in print — The Shame of the Cities (1904)
  • Reform mayors: leaders like Tom Johnson (Cleveland) and Hazen Pingree (Detroit) fought the machines
  • Australian ballot (secret ballot) reform — made it harder to directly buy and monitor votes
  • Direct primary elections: let voters rather than machine bosses choose candidates
Need a starting point?
"Citizens and reformers fought back against political machines by…"
Let's Go Deeper
Political machines were corrupt — but they also helped poor immigrants navigate a new country and find jobs. Does good service justify corrupt methods? Where do you draw the line?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what political machines were, how they worked, and why they persisted despite being corrupt. What does their existence tell you about who the government was actually serving?

Standard 2.07
Social Reform in the Progressive Era
Think About It
Industrialization created enormous wealth — and enormous suffering. What do you think it would take for ordinary people to push back against that and demand change?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Urban population and poverty, Progressive Era — rapid industrialization created vast wealth and vast suffering in the same cities. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Tenement alley, New York City, c.1910 — Jacob Riis and Lewis Hine documented conditions like these to build public support for Progressive reform. Library of Congress.
Problems of Industrial Cities
  • Overcrowded tenements: Jacob Riis documented NYC slum life in How the Other Half Lives (1890)
  • Poor sanitation, contaminated water, disease outbreaks in working-class neighborhoods
  • Political corruption: machines like Tammany Hall controlled cities through patronage
  • Child labor, long hours, dangerous working conditions — government took little action
Need a starting point?
"The problems in American industrial cities during this era included…"
Muckrakers & the Press
  • Muckrakers: journalists who exposed corruption and unsafe conditions to build public pressure for reform
  • Upton Sinclair's The Jungle (1906): exposed horrifying meatpacking conditions — led to the Pure Food and Drug Act
  • Ida Tarbell investigated and exposed Standard Oil's monopoly practices — helped break it up
  • Lincoln Steffens documented political corruption in American cities in magazine articles
Need a starting point?
"The muckrakers were important because they…"
Key Reformers
  • Jane Addams: founded Hull House in Chicago (1889) — settlement house providing services to urban poor
  • Susan B. Anthony and the suffrage movement: decades-long fight for women's right to vote
  • Theodore Roosevelt: "trust-busting" — used the Sherman Antitrust Act to break up monopolies
  • NAACP founded 1909: W.E.B. Du Bois — fought racial discrimination and pushed for federal anti-lynching law
Need a starting point?
"Reformers like Jane Addams changed America by…"
Let's Go Deeper
Progressive reformers exposed serious problems — but did they actually change the system, or just smooth over its worst edges? What would real structural change have looked like?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the problems industrial America created and the people who pushed back. Who do you think was most effective, and why? Walk through your reasoning.
