Standard 5.01
Causes of World War II
Think About It
Hitler came to power legally in a democracy. Within six years he had started the deadliest war in human history. How does something like that happen — and why did other countries wait so long to stop him?

Versailles & Appeasement
  • Treaty of Versailles humiliated Germany — war guilt, $33 billion reparations, lost territory — Hitler exploited this resentment
  • Great Depression devastated the German economy — 30% unemployment made extremism appealing
  • Appeasement: Britain and France kept giving Hitler what he demanded hoping to avoid war — Munich Agreement (1938) gave him Czechoslovakia's Sudetenland
  • Hitler took the Rhineland (1936), Austria (1938), Czechoslovakia (1939) — each time with no military response
Need a starting point?
"The policy of appeasement failed because…"
The Rise of Fascism
  • Fascism: extreme nationalism, dictatorship, rejection of democracy and individual rights
  • Italy under Mussolini (1922), Germany under Hitler (1933), Japan under military government
  • Nazi ideology: extreme nationalism, antisemitism, racial "purity" — Jews, Roma, and others blamed for Germany's problems
  • Mussolini invaded Ethiopia (1935) — League of Nations imposed weak sanctions; did nothing effective
Need a starting point?
"Fascism rose in Europe because the conditions that existed included…"
U.S. Neutrality Acts
  • Americans overwhelmingly opposed entering another European war — isolationism remained strong
  • Neutrality Acts (1935-1937): banned arms sales and loans to any warring nation — applied to aggressors and victims equally
  • Cash-and-Carry (1939): allowed arms sales if buyers paid cash and transported goods themselves — tilted toward Britain
  • Lend-Lease (1941): allowed FDR to "lend" war materials to Britain — U.S. was no longer truly neutral
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. moved from strict neutrality toward support for Britain because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Could WWII have been prevented? At what point do you think the window to stop Hitler actually closed?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Connect the Treaty of Versailles to the rise of Hitler to the failure of appeasement. What do you think was the biggest missed opportunity to stop what was coming?

Standard 5.02
The Attack on Pearl Harbor
Think About It
Japan and the U.S. were not officially at war — yet Japan launched a surprise attack killing 2,400 Americans. What had the U.S. done to push Japan toward that decision?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Japan's Pacific expansion and attack route to Pearl Harbor — Japan's empire stretched across much of Asia by December 1941, threatening American interests across the Pacific. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
USS Arizona burning at Pearl Harbor, December 7, 1941 — 1,177 crew members were killed aboard the Arizona alone. The ship still rests on the harbor floor as a memorial. Library of Congress.
U.S.-Japan Tensions
  • Japan invaded China (1937) — U.S. protested but took no military action
  • Japan joined the Axis powers (Germany, Italy) with the Tripartite Pact (1940)
  • U.S. cut off oil exports to Japan (1941) — Japan needed oil to sustain its military machine
  • U.S. froze Japanese assets in American banks — Japan faced a choice: retreat from China or find resources elsewhere
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. actions that pushed Japan toward war included…"
The Attack
  • December 7, 1941 — "a date which will live in infamy" (Roosevelt)
  • Japan launched 353 aircraft from 6 carriers — struck in two waves beginning at 7:55am
  • 2,403 Americans killed; 1,178 wounded; 19 ships damaged or sunk; 188 aircraft destroyed
  • Japanese code name: Operation Z — intended to destroy U.S. Pacific Fleet and buy time for Japan's conquests
Need a starting point?
"The attack on Pearl Harbor was significant because…"
The American Response
  • FDR asked Congress for a declaration of war December 8 — passed 82-0 in Senate, 388-1 in House
  • Germany and Italy declared war on the U.S. December 11 — U.S. now in both the Pacific and European wars
  • Japanese American internment: 120,000 forcibly relocated to camps under Executive Order 9066 (1942)
  • U.S. industry mobilized for total war — "Arsenal of Democracy" shifted to war production essentially overnight
Need a starting point?
"The United States responded to Pearl Harbor by…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. interned 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them U.S. citizens — after Pearl Harbor. How do you evaluate that decision? What does it reveal about how fear shapes civil liberties in wartime?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the path from U.S.-Japan tensions to Pearl Harbor to the American response. What do you think was the most significant long-term consequence of December 7 — beyond the immediate casualties?

Standard 5.02
D-Day and the War in Europe
Think About It
The largest seaborne invasion in history — 160,000 troops crossing the English Channel knowing thousands would die. What do you think motivated soldiers to keep moving forward under those conditions?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. troops approaching Omaha Beach, Normandy, June 6, 1944 — D-Day was the largest amphibious invasion in history. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
D-Day beaches and Allied advance through France, 1944 — the liberation of Western Europe began on June 6. Within 11 months of D-Day, Germany surrendered. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. troops approaching Omaha Beach, June 6, 1944 — D-Day was the largest amphibious invasion in history. Over 2,000 Americans died on Omaha Beach alone. Library of Congress.
Allied Planning
  • Tehran Conference (1943): FDR, Churchill, Stalin agreed — Operation Overlord would open a Western Front in France
  • Stalin had been demanding a second front since 1942 — Soviet Union suffering staggering losses on the Eastern Front
  • Operation Fortitude: elaborate deception made Germans think the real invasion would come at Calais — critical to Overlord's success
  • General Eisenhower named Supreme Commander of Allied forces — coordinated the largest military operation in history
Need a starting point?
"The Allied leaders at Tehran agreed to D-Day because…"
Operation Overlord
  • June 6, 1944 — Allied forces landed on five beaches in Normandy: Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword
  • Omaha Beach: worst fighting — U.S. forces faced steep cliffs and concentrated German fire; 2,000+ casualties on one beach
  • 156,000 Allied troops landed on June 6 — 4,414 Allied deaths confirmed that day
  • Within weeks: the beachhead was secured and Allied forces began the drive across France
Need a starting point?
"Operation Overlord succeeded despite terrible casualties because…"
Victory in Europe
  • Paris liberated August 25, 1944 — French resistance rose as Germans retreated
  • Battle of the Bulge (December 1944): Germany's last major counteroffensive — failed; broke remaining German offensive capacity
  • V-E Day: May 8, 1945 — Germany surrendered unconditionally after Hitler's suicide on April 30
  • The war in Europe had lasted nearly six years; tens of millions of civilians and soldiers were dead
Need a starting point?
"The effects of D-Day's success ultimately led to…"
Let's Go Deeper
D-Day was extraordinarily costly — thousands died in hours. Was there any other way to defeat Nazi Germany that might have been less costly, or was a direct assault like this inevitable?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through D-Day — the planning, the invasion, and what it meant for the war. Explain why it mattered beyond just being a military victory.

Standard 5.04
The Holocaust
Think About It
Six million Jews and millions of others were systematically murdered by the Nazi regime. The world knew — and did very little. Why do you think that happened?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Nazi concentration and extermination camps across Europe — the Holocaust was systematic, industrial-scale genocide carried out across an entire continent. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Survivors at Buchenwald, April 1945 — photographed by the U.S. Army Signal Corps after liberation. Eisenhower ordered the documentation to prevent denial. Library of Congress.
Who Was Targeted
  • Jews: 6 million murdered — approximately two-thirds of all European Jews
  • People with disabilities: first group subjected to mass murder — 200,000+ killed in the T4 euthanasia program
  • Roma/Sinti: 500,000 to 1.5 million murdered in the Holocaust
  • Political prisoners, LGBTQ+ individuals, Jehovah's Witnesses, Slavic peoples: millions more persecuted and killed
Need a starting point?
"The Holocaust targeted multiple groups, but the largest systematically murdered was…"
The U.S. Response
  • 1930s: U.S. immigration quotas were not relaxed — Jewish refugees largely turned away
  • MS St. Louis (1939): 937 Jewish refugees refused entry to Cuba and U.S. — returned to Europe
  • During the war: State Department officials suppressed reports of mass killings
  • War Refugee Board (1944): FDR finally created an agency to rescue Jews — helped save an estimated 200,000 lives
Need a starting point?
"The United States knew about the Holocaust but responded slowly because…"
Liberation & Aftermath
  • Allied troops liberated camps in spring 1945 — soldiers were completely unprepared for what they found
  • Eisenhower ordered German civilians to tour the camps — documented atrocities to prevent denial
  • Nuremberg Trials (1945-46): Nazi leaders prosecuted for "crimes against humanity" — established new principles in international law
  • United Nations Genocide Convention (1948): "Never Again" — defined genocide as an international crime
Need a starting point?
"The liberation of the camps and the Nuremberg Trials mattered because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The Nuremberg Trials established that "I was following orders" is not a valid defense for crimes against humanity. Why is that principle important — and does it still matter today?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about who the Holocaust targeted and what the U.S. could have done differently. What do you think the most important lesson of the Holocaust is — and how would you explain it in a conversation?

Standard 5.05
WWII on the American Home Front
Think About It
Total war meant the entire country had to participate — not just soldiers. How do you think that experience changed what Americans expected from their government and from each other?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Women defense workers, 1943 — six million women entered the industrial workforce during WWII. 'Rosie the Riveter' became the symbol of women's wartime contribution to the Arsenal of Democracy. Library of Congress.
Economic Changes
  • Unemployment ended: from 17% (1939) to 1% (1944) — WWII ended the Depression where the New Deal could not
  • War production: U.S. built 300,000 aircraft, 89,000 tanks, and 2 million trucks during the war
  • Rationing: rubber, nylon, gasoline, meat, sugar rationed — Americans made real sacrifices at home
  • Women entered the workforce: 6 million took factory jobs — transformed who did industrial work
Need a starting point?
"WWII transformed the American economy because…"
Social Changes
  • Women: took industrial jobs and served in military auxiliary roles — challenged prewar gender norms
  • African Americans: 1.2 million served in segregated units; Double V campaign — victory abroad AND at home
  • Bracero Program: 300,000 Mexican workers brought to U.S. farms and railroads to fill labor shortage
  • Great Migration accelerated: Black Americans continued moving north and west for factory jobs
Need a starting point?
"WWII changed American society by creating new roles for…"
Civil Liberties
  • Executive Order 9066 (1942): authorized internment of Japanese Americans — 120,000 forced into camps
  • Korematsu v. United States (1944): Supreme Court upheld internment as constitutional — later formally reversed in 1988
  • Internees lost homes, businesses, and property — given $25 and a bus ticket when released
  • German and Italian Americans also faced discrimination and surveillance — but not mass internment
Need a starting point?
"Executive Order 9066 violated civil liberties because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. fought Nazi racism in Europe while maintaining a segregated military and interning Japanese Americans at home. How do you make sense of that contradiction?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe what the home front looked like during WWII — who worked, what changed, what was sacrificed. Then address the internment: how do you evaluate that decision?

Standard 5.06
How WWII Ended and Its Consequences
Think About It
Dropping atomic bombs on two Japanese cities killed over 200,000 people — most of them civilians. Was that justified? This is one of the most debated decisions in American history. What do you think?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The atomic bomb mushroom cloud over Nagasaki, August 9, 1945 — three days after Hiroshima, the second bomb killed an estimated 40,000 people instantly. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Europe's post-war division, 1945 — the wartime alliance between the U.S. and USSR fractured almost immediately, dividing Europe into competing spheres of influence. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Atomic bomb mushroom cloud over Nagasaki, August 9, 1945 — three days after Hiroshima, the second bomb killed an estimated 40,000 people instantly. Japan surrendered six days later. Library of Congress.
Ending the Pacific War
  • V-E Day: May 8, 1945 — Germany surrendered after Hitler's suicide; war in Europe over
  • Island-hopping: fierce battles at Iwo Jima and Okinawa — U.S. planners estimated 500,000–1 million Allied casualties in a land invasion of Japan
  • Atomic bomb: Manhattan Project had secretly developed atomic weapons since 1942
  • Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9), 1945 — Japan surrendered August 15; V-J Day formally September 2
Need a starting point?
"The decision to use atomic bombs on Japan was controversial because…"
War Crimes Trials
  • Nuremberg Trials (1945-1946): 24 Nazi leaders prosecuted — 12 sentenced to death
  • Tokyo Trials (1946-1948): Japanese military leaders tried for war crimes in Asia
  • Established "crimes against humanity" as a legal category — you cannot claim following orders as a defense
  • Nuremberg principles: individuals are accountable under international law, even heads of state
Need a starting point?
"The Nuremberg Trials were historically significant because they established…"
A New World Order
  • Yalta Conference (1945): FDR, Churchill, Stalin agreed on post-war Europe — Soviet domination of Eastern Europe became clear
  • United Nations: founded April 1945 — replaced the failed League of Nations; U.S. joined this time
  • Bretton Woods (1944): created the World Bank and IMF — U.S. dollar became the world reserve currency
  • Rise of superpowers: U.S. and Soviet Union emerged dominant — setting the stage for the Cold War
Need a starting point?
"The end of WWII created a new world order because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. used atomic weapons against civilian populations in Japan — but the Nuremberg Trials established that targeting civilians is a crime. How do you think about that tension?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through how WWII ended in Europe and in the Pacific. Then think about what kind of world it left behind: what new institutions, what new dangers, what new balance of power?
