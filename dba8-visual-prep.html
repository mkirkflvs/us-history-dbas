Standard 8.01
U.S. & the Middle East (1970s-1990s)
Think About It
The Middle East kept pulling the U.S. in — oil, hostages, arms deals, proxy wars. What do you think America's real interests were in the region, and how well did U.S. policy actually serve them?

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Gas lines during the 1973 oil embargo — the Arab oil embargo revealed how dependent the U.S. had become on Middle Eastern oil, quadrupling gas prices and triggering the worst energy crisis in American history. Library of Congress.
1970s Crises
  • Yom Kippur War (1973): Egypt and Syria attacked Israel — U.S. airlifted supplies to Israel; Arab nations imposed oil embargo; gasoline prices quadrupled overnight
  • Iranian Revolution (1979): U.S.-backed Shah overthrown; Ayatollah Khomeini established an Islamic republic hostile to the U.S.
  • Iran Hostage Crisis (1979-1981): 52 American diplomats held hostage for 444 days — deeply humiliated Carter; hostages released the day Reagan was inaugurated
  • Soviet Invasion of Afghanistan (1979): U.S. funded Afghan mujahideen to fight Soviets — Cold War proxy conflict with long-term consequences
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. relationship with the Middle East became deeply complicated in the 1970s because…"
1980s Conflicts
  • Iran-Contra Affair: Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran and funneled money to Nicaraguan Contras — illegal; a major scandal
  • Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988): U.S. backed Saddam Hussein's Iraq against Iran — then later went to war against Hussein twice
  • Lebanon: 241 U.S. Marines killed in a Beirut terrorist bombing (1983) — Reagan withdrew forces shortly after
  • Reagan Doctrine: U.S. actively supported anti-communist movements worldwide, including the Afghan mujahideen
Need a starting point?
"The Iran-Contra Affair mattered because it revealed that…"
Presidents of the Era
  • Reagan (1981-1989): tax cuts ("Reaganomics"), massive defense spending increase, Cold War escalation, Iran-Contra
  • H.W. Bush (1989-1993): Gulf War (1991) — expelled Iraq from Kuwait; managed the end of the Cold War and the fall of the Soviet Union
  • Clinton (1993-2001): economic boom, NAFTA, welfare reform, budget surplus; Monica Lewinsky scandal; impeached but not removed
  • All three navigated a world being reshaped by the Cold War's end and the acceleration of globalization
Need a starting point?
"Reagan's domestic economic policy of "Reaganomics" was built on the idea that…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. armed Saddam Hussein in the 1980s — then fought two wars against him in 1991 and 2003. The U.S. armed the Afghan mujahideen — some later joined Al-Qaeda. What does that pattern tell you about the unintended consequences of foreign policy?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through U.S. involvement in the Middle East from the 1970s through the 1990s. Pick one crisis and explain what the U.S. did, why it did it, and how it turned out.

Standard 8.02
Terrorism and America's Response
Think About It
September 11, 2001 killed 2,977 Americans and permanently changed U.S. foreign policy and daily life. How should a democracy respond to terrorism — and where do you draw the line between security and freedom?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Afghanistan and the War on Terror — Operation Enduring Freedom began October 7, 2001. It became America's longest war, ending with withdrawal in August 2021. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The World Trade Center on September 11, 2001 — 2,977 people were killed in the deadliest terrorist attack in U.S. history. The attacks permanently changed U.S. foreign policy and daily life. Library of Congress.
September 11, 2001
  • Al-Qaeda: Osama bin Laden's organization — trained in Afghanistan under Taliban rule; motivated by U.S. military presence in Saudi Arabia
  • 19 hijackers seized 4 airplanes — 2 struck the World Trade Center, 1 the Pentagon, 1 crashed in Pennsylvania
  • 2,977 killed — largest loss of life from a foreign attack on U.S. soil since Pearl Harbor
  • Bush declared a "War on Terror" — an open-ended conflict against non-state actors, not a specific country
Need a starting point?
"The September 11 attacks changed America because…"
The U.S. Response
  • Invasion of Afghanistan (October 2001): toppled the Taliban government that had harbored Al-Qaeda
  • Department of Homeland Security created 2002: largest reorganization of the federal government since WWII
  • Invasion of Iraq (March 2003): Bush claimed Saddam Hussein had WMDs — none were found; sectarian civil war followed
  • Osama bin Laden killed in Pakistan by Navy SEAL Team 6, May 2, 2011 — under President Obama
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. response to 9/11 included invading two countries because…"
Civil Liberties vs. Security
  • PATRIOT Act (2001): greatly expanded government surveillance powers — monitoring phone calls, emails, and financial records
  • Guantanamo Bay: held "enemy combatants" without trial — controversy over indefinite detention and interrogation techniques
  • NSA mass surveillance: revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013 — government had been collecting data on millions of ordinary Americans
  • Core question: how much freedom should Americans give up in exchange for security — and who decides when the government has gone too far?
Need a starting point?
"The debate over civil liberties after 9/11 centered on the question of…"
Let's Go Deeper
The PATRIOT Act gave the government sweeping new surveillance powers justified by the need to prevent terrorism. How do you think about that tradeoff between security and privacy — and who should decide where the line is?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through 9/11, the U.S. response, and the civil liberties debate. What do you think was the most significant long-term consequence of September 11 on American society?

Environmental Concerns in Modern America
Think About It
Environmental disasters often strike suddenly — a spill, a meltdown, a wildfire. But the causes build slowly over decades. What does it take for a society to address slow-building problems before they become crises?

Oil Spills & Nuclear Accidents
  • Exxon Valdez oil spill (1989): tanker ran aground off Alaska — 11 million gallons of oil; 1,300 miles of coastline devastated; recovery took decades
  • Three Mile Island (1979): partial nuclear meltdown in Pennsylvania — no immediate deaths but ended U.S. nuclear plant construction for 30 years
  • BP Deepwater Horizon (2010): explosion in the Gulf of Mexico — 11 workers killed; 4.9 million barrels of oil released; massive ecosystem damage
  • Santa Barbara oil spill (1969): helped inspire the first Earth Day (1970) and the Clean Water Act (1972)
Need a starting point?
"Environmental disasters like the Exxon Valdez mattered because they showed…"
Climate Change
  • Scientific consensus: burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ — greenhouse effect is warming the planet; every major scientific organization agrees
  • Projected effects: rising sea levels, more intense hurricanes and wildfires, drought, species extinction
  • Paris Agreement (2015): international agreement to limit warming — U.S. joined under Obama, withdrew under Trump, rejoined under Biden
  • Policy debate: who is responsible — governments, corporations, individuals? How do we balance economic costs with environmental necessity?
Need a starting point?
"Climate change is difficult to address politically because…"
Space Exploration
  • Apollo 11 (July 1969): Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin — first humans on the moon; fulfilled Kennedy's 1961 challenge
  • Space Shuttle program (1981-2011): reusable spacecraft; Challenger (1986) and Columbia (2003) disasters killed 14 astronauts
  • International Space Station (1998-present): joint U.S.-Russian project — cooperation during ongoing Cold War tensions
  • SpaceX (2020): first private company to send humans to orbit — opened a new era of commercial spaceflight
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. space program was driven by both scientific goals and…"
Let's Go Deeper
Environmental protection and economic growth are often framed as opposites. Do you agree with that framing? What would it actually look like to prioritize both at the same time?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the environmental challenges America has faced — from oil spills to climate change. Pick one and explain what happened, what was done about it, and what you think the long-term consequences are.

Standard 8.03
Social and Health Issues in Modern America
Think About It
How a society responds to a public health crisis reveals its values — who it protects, who it ignores, and what it's willing to invest in. What do the AIDS crisis and COVID-19 tell us about American values?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The AIDS Memorial Quilt on the National Mall, Washington D.C. — each panel represents someone who died of AIDS. The quilt has grown to over 50,000 panels, making it the largest ongoing folk art project in the world. Library of Congress.
The War on Drugs
  • Nixon declared "War on Drugs" in 1971 — a Nixon aide later admitted it was partly designed to target anti-war activists and Black communities
  • Reagan escalated: "Just Say No," mandatory minimum sentences; crack cocaine penalties 100:1 compared to powder cocaine
  • Mass incarceration: U.S. prison population grew from 300,000 (1980) to 2.3 million (2008) — disproportionately affected Black and Latino Americans
  • Opioid epidemic (2000s-present): pharmaceutical companies aggressively marketed prescription painkillers; 500,000+ overdose deaths since 1999
Need a starting point?
"The War on Drugs had disproportionate effects on communities of color because…"
The HIV/AIDS Crisis
  • AIDS identified 1981 — Reagan administration slow to respond; over 20,000 Americans had died before Reagan publicly addressed it in 1987
  • ACT UP: AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power — aggressive activism forced faster drug approval processes and more research funding
  • Ryan White: 13-year-old hemophiliac who died of AIDS (1990) — humanized the epidemic; Ryan White CARE Act expanded treatment access
  • Antiretroviral drugs (1996) transformed HIV from a death sentence to a manageable chronic condition
Need a starting point?
"The government's slow response to the AIDS crisis revealed…"
Health Policy & COVID-19
  • Affordable Care Act (2010): expanded health insurance coverage to 20 million uninsured Americans — faced years of political and legal challenges
  • COVID-19 pandemic (2020): over one million Americans died — the deadliest disease event in U.S. history since the 1918 flu
  • mRNA vaccine development in under a year: a scientific achievement; vaccine hesitancy became a major political and public health challenge
  • COVID-19 killed Black, Latino, and Native American communities at far higher rates — existing health inequalities were exposed and amplified
Need a starting point?
"The COVID-19 pandemic revealed existing inequalities in American healthcare because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Every public health crisis we've studied — AIDS, the opioid epidemic, COVID-19 — hit marginalized communities harder than others. What do you think that consistent pattern tells us about American society?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare two of the health crises we've discussed. How did the government respond to each? What determined who got help and who didn't? What does that tell you about American values?

Standard 8.03
Immigration and Social Change
Think About It
Every generation of Americans has debated immigration — who should be allowed in, how many, on what terms. Why do you think this question never gets permanently settled?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
New Americans at a naturalization ceremony — the U.S. naturalizes approximately 900,000 new citizens each year, continuing a tradition that has defined the nation since its founding. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Modern U.S. immigration — since the Immigration Act of 1965, immigration has shifted from Europe to Latin America, Asia, and Africa, fundamentally changing American demographics. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
New Americans at a citizenship naturalization ceremony — the U.S. naturalizes approximately 900,000 new citizens each year. Library of Congress.
Immigration Policy
  • Immigration Act of 1965: eliminated national-origin quotas; prioritized family reunification and skills — transformed who could come to America
  • Immigration Reform and Control Act (1986): Reagan's amnesty for 3 million undocumented immigrants; also criminalized hiring undocumented workers
  • DACA (2012): Obama's executive order protected undocumented immigrants brought as children from deportation — about 700,000 "Dreamers"
  • Comprehensive immigration reform has repeatedly failed in Congress — political gridlock on a major national challenge
Need a starting point?
"The Immigration Act of 1965 transformed American immigration because…"
Cultural Change
  • U.S. is one of the most diverse nations in history — no single ethnic or racial majority is expected by 2050
  • Latino population: 62 million (19% of population) — the largest minority group; Spanish is the second language of the U.S.
  • Asian American population: fastest-growing demographic — highly diverse, including Southeast Asian refugees and tech industry workers
  • American food, music, and culture are continuously reshaped by immigrant communities in every generation
Need a starting point?
"Immigration has changed American culture by…"
Ongoing Debates
  • Border security vs. humanitarian obligation: how should the U.S. handle asylum seekers fleeing gang violence, political persecution, or poverty?
  • Undocumented population: estimated 11 million undocumented immigrants — deportation vs. path to citizenship is the central policy divide
  • Economic arguments: immigrants fill crucial roles in agriculture, construction, and healthcare — but wage competition concerns persist
  • National identity debate: who is "American"? The immigration debate is at its core a debate about what America is meant to be
Need a starting point?
"The immigration debate is really a debate about American national identity because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. is often called "a nation of immigrants" — yet immigration restriction has been a constant theme throughout American history. How do you make sense of that contradiction?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through how U.S. immigration policy has changed since 1965 and explain the cultural shifts those changes produced. Then explain what you think is really at the heart of the ongoing debate.

Standard 8.04
Globalization and Economic Change
Think About It
Your phone was designed in California, assembled in China, with parts from a dozen countries. What does it mean for American workers and communities when the economy is truly global?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American manufacturing workers on an assembly line — automation and global trade have dramatically reshaped manufacturing employment over the past four decades. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Global trade routes in the modern era — the U.S. economy is deeply interconnected with production, supply chains, and markets worldwide. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American manufacturing workers — automation and global trade have dramatically reshaped manufacturing employment over the past four decades, eliminating millions of jobs in the Rust Belt. Library of Congress.
Reaganomics & Inflation
  • 1970s stagflation: high inflation + high unemployment at the same time — contradicted standard economic models
  • Volcker Shock (1979-1982): Fed Chairman Paul Volcker raised interest rates to 20% to break inflation — caused recession but eventually worked
  • Reaganomics: tax cuts (top rate from 70% to 28%), deregulation, reduced domestic spending, increased defense spending — "supply-side economics"
  • Results: recovery in the mid-1980s, but income inequality widened sharply — the gap between rich and poor grew significantly
Need a starting point?
"Reagan's economic approach differed from previous policies because…"
Trade Agreements
  • NAFTA (1994): Clinton's free trade agreement with Canada and Mexico — eliminated most tariffs; U.S. manufacturing moved to Mexico for cheaper labor
  • WTO (1995): global framework for trade rules; China joined in 2001
  • China trade: U.S.-China trade grew massively — cheap goods for consumers; an estimated 2-2.4 million manufacturing jobs left the U.S.
  • Free trade vs. fair trade debate: globalization benefited consumers and investors but devastated specific industries and communities
Need a starting point?
"NAFTA and trade with China affected American workers by…"
Impact on Americans
  • Winners: consumers (cheaper goods), college-educated workers (global markets for their skills), investors (global corporate profits)
  • Losers: manufacturing workers (plant closures), agricultural communities (foreign competition), specific regions (the Rust Belt)
  • Income inequality: the top 1%'s share of income grew from 10% (1980) to 20% (2010) — largest wealth concentration since the 1920s
  • Political consequences: working-class economic anxiety drove political polarization — visible in both Trump's and Sanders' 2016 campaigns
Need a starting point?
"Globalization's impact was uneven because it helped some Americans while harming others, specifically…"
Let's Go Deeper
Globalization made everyday goods cheaper for American consumers — but eliminated millions of manufacturing jobs. How do you evaluate that tradeoff? Is there a way to get both the benefits and protect the workers?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what globalization is and walk through its impact on American workers and communities. Be specific: who benefited, who was hurt, and what were the political consequences of that divide?

Standard 8.05
Human Rights & Nuclear Proliferation
Think About It
The U.S. has often declared itself the world's defender of human rights and democracy — but its record is complicated. How do you evaluate a country that champions human rights abroad while struggling to fulfill them at home?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Protesters demanding rights and justice in Washington D.C., 1960s — America has both championed human rights internationally and struggled to fulfill them domestically, a tension that shapes U.S. credibility worldwide. Library of Congress.
Human Rights Efforts
  • UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948): set global standards — still the foundation of international human rights law
  • Helsinki Accords (1975): U.S., Soviet Union, and European nations agreed to respect human rights — gave Soviet dissidents a legal basis to demand rights
  • Carter Doctrine: Carter made human rights a central element of U.S. foreign policy — criticized both allies and adversaries
  • Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch: non-government organizations that document abuses and build international pressure
Need a starting point?
"International human rights efforts have been significant but limited because…"
Nuclear Proliferation
  • Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968): nations with nukes agreed not to share technology; others agreed not to develop it — major powers kept their arsenals
  • Cold War's end did not end nuclear danger — Russia, China, UK, France, India, Pakistan, Israel, and North Korea all have nuclear weapons
  • North Korea tested its first nuclear device in 2006 — continued developing missiles capable of reaching the U.S.
  • Iran nuclear program: ongoing negotiations to limit Iran's nuclear development in exchange for eased sanctions
Need a starting point?
"Nuclear proliferation remains a global challenge because…"
America's Role
  • U.S. remains the world's largest military spender — about 38% of global military spending
  • "Responsibility to Protect" (R2P): debated international principle that the global community can intervene when governments commit atrocities against their own citizens
  • Tensions between U.S. values and interests: the U.S. has supported authoritarian governments when strategically convenient
  • America's domestic struggles with racial justice and inequality affect its credibility as a human rights champion internationally
Need a starting point?
"The tension in America's role as a human rights champion is that…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. claims to be a defender of human rights globally — but has also supported authoritarian governments when it served strategic interests. Does that hypocrisy undermine the U.S.'s ability to promote human rights effectively?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about U.S. human rights policy and nuclear proliferation. What do you think is America's responsibility in a world where rights are violated and nuclear weapons continue to spread?
