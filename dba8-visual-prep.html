Standard 8.01
The U.S. and the Middle East
Think About It
The Middle East keeps pulling the U.S. in, oil, hostages, arms deals, proxy wars. What are America's actual interests there, and has U.S. policy served them?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Gas lines during the 1973 oil embargo — the most powerful country in the world, halted by a foreign oil policy decision. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Vietnam — the same logic that drove U.S. involvement in Southeast Asia shaped Middle East policy. Short-term tactical alliances created long-term strategic disasters. Library of Congress.
U.S. involvement in the Middle East accelerated dramatically in the 1970s and 1980s, driven by oil dependency, Cold War competition, and a series of crises that revealed how dependent American prosperity was on a region it barely understood. The Iran hostage crisis, the Iran-Contra scandal, and supporting Saddam Hussein against Iran, the contradictions accumulated fast.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Energy Crisis
The 1973 oil embargo quadrupled gas prices and revealed that the American economy ran on Middle Eastern oil. Suddenly, regional politics thousands of miles away could shut down American highways.
›
Step 2
The Backfires
The U.S. armed Saddam Hussein against Iran in the 1980s, then fought two wars against him in 1991 and 2003. The U.S. funded the Afghan mujahideen against the Soviets, some became al-Qaeda.
›
Step 3
The Pattern
Short-term tactical alliances based on Cold War logic repeatedly created long-term strategic disasters. The region's complexity consistently defeated American assumptions about how to control it.
🔗
Real World Connection
The gas price crisis of 1973 created the first push for alternative energy in the U.S. When that pressure eased, the push faded. Then 2008 oil prices, then Ukraine, then each new crisis restarts the same conversation. The pattern has been repeating for 50 years.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"U.S. involvement in the Middle East was driven by... but kept backfiring because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. armed people who became its enemies twice in the 1980s. Saddam Hussein and the Afghan mujahideen. What does that pattern tell you about the limits of short-term foreign policy thinking?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through U.S. involvement in the Middle East from the 1970s through the 1990s. Pick one crisis and explain what the U.S. did, why it did it, and how it turned out.

Standard 8.02
September 11 and Its Aftermath
Think About It
2,977 people were killed on September 11, 2001. The decisions made in the weeks and months after shaped the world you live in. What trade-offs were made, and were they worth it?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The World Trade Center, September 11, 2001 — 2,977 people killed in the deadliest terrorist attack in U.S. history. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
McCarthy, 1954 — the post-9/11 PATRIOT Act and NSA surveillance followed the same pattern as McCarthyism: use national security fear to expand government power over civil liberties. Library of Congress.
September 11 was the deadliest foreign attack on American soil since Pearl Harbor and it changed everything: foreign policy, airport security, surveillance, civil liberties. A whole generation's understanding of what it means to feel safe. The response (two wars, the PATRIOT Act, Guantanamo, NSA surveillance) lasted far longer and cost far more than anyone anticipated.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Attack
19 hijackers, four planes, 2,977 dead. Al-Qaeda, led by Osama bin Laden and based in Taliban-controlled Afghanistan, had been planning this for years. U.S. intelligence failures contributed.
›
Step 2
The Response
Afghanistan (2001): toppled the Taliban. Iraq (2003): based on false claims about WMDs. Both became long, costly occupations. bin Laden wasn't killed until 2011 in Pakistan.
›
Step 3
The Trade-Off
The PATRIOT Act expanded surveillance dramatically. NSA collected data on millions of Americans. Guantanamo held people indefinitely without trial. The question: how much freedom do you give up for security?
🔗
Real World Connection
You've gone through airport security your whole life, that's post-9/11. Every time you take your shoes off at security, that's a decision made in 2001. Still in effect. So is the surveillance infrastructure revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The September 11 attacks changed America by... including decisions that are still controversial because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The PATRIOT Act gave the government sweeping surveillance powers. How do you decide how much freedom to trade for security, and who should make that call?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through 9/11, the U.S. response, and the civil liberties debate. What do you think was the most significant long-term consequence on American society?

Standard 8.03
The Environment: Slow Crisis, Fast Disasters
Think About It
Environmental disasters happen suddenly, but the causes build for decades. Why is it so hard for societies to address slow-building problems before they become catastrophes?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Deepwater Horizon oil spill, 2010 — the largest marine oil spill in U.S. history. 4.9 million barrels over 87 days. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Hooverville, Ohio, 1936 — environmental disasters and economic collapses follow the same pattern: slow-building structural causes, sudden visible crisis, incomplete recovery. Library of Congress.
From the Exxon Valdez to the Deepwater Horizon to climate change, environmental challenges keep outrunning political responses. The pattern is consistent: disaster strikes, there's outrage and reform, then the pressure eases and things revert. Climate change is the ultimate version of this pattern, the timeline for action is decades, the timeline for politics is two to four years.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Disasters
Exxon Valdez (1989) and Deepwater Horizon (2010) devastated ecosystems and forced new regulations. Three Mile Island (1979) ended nuclear plant construction for 30 years. Each disaster reshaped policy, temporarily.
›
Step 2
The Slow Crisis
Climate change is different: the cause-and-effect connection is real but distributed across decades. Scientific consensus has been clear for 30 years. Political action has been inconsistent.
›
Step 3
The Complexity
Energy policy involves jobs, geopolitics, corporate interests, and genuine technological limitations. There's no simple answer, but the cost of inaction keeps rising.
🔗
Real World Connection
The first Earth Day (1970) came after the Santa Barbara oil spill. The Clean Air Act followed. Then oil prices rose, conservation pressure mounted, and eventually eased. The same cycle has played out repeatedly. Understanding this pattern helps you understand why climate action has been so difficult.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"Environmental policy in the U.S. has generally only changed after... which suggests..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Environmental protection and economic growth are often framed as opposites. Is that framing accurate, or is it manufactured by interests who benefit from keeping them in conflict?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the environmental challenges America has faced. Pick one and explain what happened, what was done, and what the long-term consequences are.

Standard 8.03
Public Health as a Mirror of Society
Think About It
Every public health crisis in American history has hit marginalized communities hardest. Is that a coincidence, or does it reveal something consistent about how the system works?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The AIDS Memorial Quilt on the National Mall — each panel represents someone who died. The quilt turned individual deaths into collective grief visible at a national scale. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Supreme Court — every public health crisis eventually reaches the courts. From vaccine mandates to abortion rights to AIDS treatment funding. Library of Congress.
The AIDS crisis, the opioid epidemic, and COVID-19 all followed the same pattern: initial indifference or active hostility from government, communities organizing to demand a response, and an eventual reckoning that came too late for too many people. In each case, who got help and when was shaped by race, class, and politics as much as by medicine.
How It Connects
Step 1
The AIDS Crisis
Reagan didn't publicly address AIDS until 1987. By then, 20,000 Americans had already died. The activist organization ACT UP used the Civil Rights Movement's direct-action tactics to force faster drug approvals.
›
Step 2
The Opioid Epidemic
Pharmaceutical companies pushed opioid prescriptions aggressively. 500,000+ overdose deaths since 1999. The response, and the legal accountability, came decades late and is still incomplete.
›
Step 3
COVID-19
COVID killed over a million Americans. Black, Latino, and Native American communities died at significantly higher rates, existing health inequalities exposed and amplified by a new disease.
🔗
Real World Connection
The pattern of whose suffering gets taken seriously, and when, runs through all of these crises. ACT UP's tactics, like staging 'die-ins' in front of government buildings,, disrupting FDA hearings, were derived directly from the Civil Rights Movement. Desperation invents the same tactics across generations.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"Public health crises in America tend to be handled worse for... because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The government responded slowly to AIDS, opioids, and COVID-19, and in each case, the slow response killed people. What does that pattern tell you about American public health priorities?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare two health crises. How did the government respond to each? What determined who got help and when?

Standard 8.03
Immigration: The Debate That Never Ends
Think About It
Every generation of Americans has debated immigration. Why does this question never get permanently settled?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
New Americans at a naturalization ceremony — the U.S. naturalizes about 900,000 new citizens each year. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Immigrants at Ellis Island, early 1900s — the arguments against their arrival are nearly word-for-word the same as immigration debates today. Library of Congress.
The Immigration Act of 1965 transformed who could come to America, shifting from European quotas to family reunification and skills. The result, over 60 years, has been the most diverse nation in human history. And yet the debate over immigration has never been more contentious. Because at its core, the immigration debate is really a debate about what America is.
How It Connects
Step 1
What Changed
Before 1965: immigration heavily favored Northern Europeans. After 1965: Latin American, Asian, and African immigration transformed American demographics. By 2045, no single racial or ethnic group will be a majority.
›
Step 2
What Stayed the Same
The arguments against immigration in 2024 are nearly identical to those in 1924: cultural change, job competition, crime, failure to assimilate. The fears are consistently the same even when the targeted group changes.
›
Step 3
What's Actually at Stake
Immigration debates are really identity debates, about what America is, who belongs, and whose vision of the country wins. The demographics are changing. The question is whether the idea of America changes with them.
🔗
Real World Connection
The United States is often called "a nation of immigrants", but it was also built on land taken from Native Americans by enslaved labor. Holding all of that at once is what it means to honestly grapple with American identity. The immigration debate is where that complexity becomes unavoidable.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The immigration debate keeps repeating because it's really a debate about..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. calls itself a "nation of immigrants" but has repeatedly tried to limit immigration. Is that a contradiction, or does it reveal something consistent about how America thinks about belonging?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through how U.S. immigration policy has changed and what the cultural shifts have been. Then explain what you think is really at the heart of the ongoing debate.

Standard 8.04
Globalization: Who Won and Who Lost
Think About It
Your phone was designed in California, assembled in China, with parts from a dozen countries, using code written in India. What does it mean for American workers when the economy is truly global?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American manufacturing workers — automation and global trade have gutted the communities built around this work. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Ford's assembly line, c.1913 — American industrial supremacy was built on mass production. Globalization transferred that advantage overseas. Library of Congress.
Globalization since the 1980s created real benefits, cheaper goods, global markets, innovation, and real damage: millions of manufacturing jobs eliminated, Rust Belt communities hollowed out, income inequality reaching levels not seen since the 1920s. The political consequences are still playing out.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Policy
Reagan's supply-side economics cut taxes on the wealthy, deregulated industries, and reduced unions' power. NAFTA (1994) and China's WTO entry (2001) accelerated manufacturing job losses.
›
Step 2
The Winners
Consumers got cheaper goods. College-educated workers in tech, finance, and healthcare thrived. Corporate profits soared. Investors did genuinely well.
›
Step 3
The Losers
Manufacturing workers in the Rust Belt lost jobs that never came back. The top 1%'s share of income doubled from 10% to 20% between 1980 and 2010. The political anger this created reshaped both parties.
🔗
Real World Connection
The political upheaval of the 2010s and 2020s. Sanders, Trump, polarization, the collapse of the political center, is directly connected to the economic dislocation of globalization. When people feel the economy has abandoned them and the political system hasn't responded, they look for different answers. Understanding the economic history helps explain the political present.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"Globalization benefited some Americans while hurting others, specifically..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Free trade makes goods cheaper for consumers but eliminates jobs for some workers. How do you evaluate that trade-off, and who should decide?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what globalization is and its impact on American workers and communities. Who benefited, who was hurt, and what were the political consequences?

Standard 8.05
America's Role: Promise vs. Reality
Think About It
The U.S. declares itself the world's defender of democracy and human rights. But it's also supported dictators, used torture, and struggled to deliver rights at home. How do you evaluate a country that doesn't live up to its own values?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Protesters demanding rights in Washington D.C. — America promotes human rights internationally while still fighting over them at home. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Jim Crow — the gap between what America promises and what it delivers is most visible at home. Other countries point back at this history when the U.S. lectures them on human rights. Library of Congress.
America's role in the world rests on a claimed moral authority, we stand for freedom, democracy, human rights. That claim has always been complicated by the gap between rhetoric and reality: supporting authoritarian allies, overlooking atrocities by friendly governments, and the ongoing domestic failures visible in every civil rights era. The gap between what America says and what America does shapes how the rest of the world sees it.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Ideal
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), the Nuremberg principles, U.S. support for democracy movements worldwide. America has genuinely championed human rights, at times and in places.
›
Step 2
The Gap
The U.S. supported apartheid South Africa until very late. It backed Pinochet in Chile, Saddam Hussein in Iraq, the Shah in Iran. Strategic interests repeatedly overrode stated values.
›
Step 3
The Credibility
Nuclear proliferation. North Korea, Iran, shows the limits of American power. When the U.S. fails to practice what it preaches at home, its moral authority to demand accountability abroad shrinks.
🔗
Real World Connection
When you see the U.S. criticize another country's human rights record, other countries often point back at American failures, police violence, mass incarceration, the history of slavery. That's not whataboutism: it's the real cost of the credibility gap. The question of how America talks about its values while failing to fully live them is one of the defining tensions of American foreign policy.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"America's credibility as a human rights champion is complicated by..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. has often framed its foreign policy in human rights terms while supporting authoritarian regimes when strategically convenient. Is that hypocrisy, or is it just how international relations work?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about America's role in the world. What do you think the U.S. gets right, and where does the gap between the ideal and the reality matter most?
