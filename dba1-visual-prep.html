Standard 1.01
Causes of the Civil War
Think About It
People in 1860 knew war was coming — but they disagreed about what it was really about. What do you think was the deepest cause of the Civil War?

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thomas Nast, 'Worse Than Slavery,' Harper's Weekly 1874 — the KKK and White League crush Reconstruction's promise. Library of Congress.
Economic Differences
  • North: industrialized, wage labor, diversified economy
  • South: plantation agriculture, slave labor, cotton dependent
  • Tariffs benefited Northern manufacturing but hurt Southern planters who bought imports
  • South saw Northern economic dominance as a threat to its way of life
Need a starting point?
"The economic differences between North and South caused conflict because…"
Political Tensions
  • Expansion of slavery into new territories — would new states be free or slave?
  • Compromise of 1850 and Kansas-Nebraska Act temporarily papered over divisions
  • "Bleeding Kansas": pro-slavery and anti-slavery settlers fought a guerrilla war over Kansas statehood
  • Election of Lincoln (1860): South saw a Republican president as an existential threat
Need a starting point?
"The political conflict over slavery's expansion led to war because…"
Slavery
  • 3.9 million enslaved people in the South by 1860 — the entire Southern economy depended on their labor
  • Abolition movement in the North — Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin (1852) spread anti-slavery sentiment
  • Dred Scott decision (1857): Supreme Court ruled enslaved people were not citizens and had no rights
  • John Brown's raid on Harper's Ferry (1859): convinced many Southerners that the North wanted slave rebellion
Need a starting point?
"Slavery was the central cause of the Civil War because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Some historians argue states' rights were the real cause of the Civil War. Others say it was always about slavery. What does the historical evidence tell you?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through what you think were the deepest causes of the Civil War. Don't just list them — explain how they connected to each other.

Standard 1.02
Consequences of the Civil War
Think About It
The Civil War killed more Americans than any other conflict in U.S. history — and it didn't just destroy lives; it destroyed and rebuilt entire systems. What do you think changed most fundamentally?

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Richmond, Virginia in ruins, April 1865 — the Confederate capital burned during the evacuation. Library of Congress.
Human Cost
  • 620,000–750,000 soldiers died — more than all other American wars combined until Vietnam
  • Hundreds of thousands more wounded, many permanently disabled
  • Widows, orphans, and destroyed families across both North and South
  • 4 million enslaved people freed — but freedom came without land, money, or legal protection
Need a starting point?
"The human cost of the Civil War was staggering because…"
Economic Impact
  • South's agricultural economy destroyed — plantations burned, infrastructure wrecked, Confederate currency worthless
  • Freedpeople left slavery with nothing — no land, no capital, no education
  • North's industrial economy boomed during the war — manufacturing capacity expanded dramatically
  • Sharecropping emerged: former slaves technically free but economically bound to white landowners
Need a starting point?
"The economic consequences of the Civil War were very different for the North and South because…"
Political Changes
  • 13th Amendment (1865): abolished slavery permanently
  • Federal government's power expanded — the Union victory settled the question of federal vs. state authority
  • Reconstruction began: question of how to readmit Southern states and define freedom for Black Americans
  • Radical Republicans vs. President Johnson: who controls Reconstruction?
Need a starting point?
"The most important political consequence of the Civil War was…"
Let's Go Deeper
The Civil War ended slavery — but within a generation, a new system of racial oppression emerged in the South. What does that tell you about the limits of military victory?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Describe the consequences of the Civil War across human, economic, and political dimensions. Which consequence do you think was most significant in shaping what came next?

Standard 1.03
Influence on Reconstruction
Think About It
The South had just lost a war to preserve slavery. Who should decide how it rejoins the Union — and on what terms? The answer to that question defined Reconstruction.
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thaddeus Stevens, Radical Republican congressman — Stevens argued that the South had forfeited its rights through rebellion and should be reconstructed as a democracy. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The five Reconstruction Military Districts — military rule and required Black male suffrage before states could rejoin the Union. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thaddeus Stevens, Radical Republican leader — Stevens argued the South had forfeited its rights through rebellion and must be reconstructed as a democracy. Library of Congress.
Presidential Reconstruction
  • Lincoln's "10 Percent Plan": if 10% of voters swore loyalty, a state could rejoin the Union
  • Johnson's plan: quick readmission, little protection for freedpeople, no redistribution of land
  • Southern states passed Black Codes immediately — laws restricting where Black people could live, work, and travel
  • Congress rejected Johnson's lenient approach — passed its own Reconstruction over his vetoes
Need a starting point?
"Presidential Reconstruction was considered too lenient by Radical Republicans because…"
Radical Reconstruction
  • Reconstruction Act (1867): divided the South into 5 military districts — required new state constitutions
  • Required states to ratify the 14th Amendment before readmission
  • Freedmen's Bureau: provided food, education, legal help to 4 million formerly enslaved people
  • Black men voted and held office — 16 Black congressmen, 2 Black senators during Reconstruction
Need a starting point?
"Radical Reconstruction was different from Presidential Reconstruction because it…"
Freedpeople's Agency
  • Freed people immediately sought literacy — established schools and churches as centers of community life
  • Sought land ("40 acres and a mule") — land redistribution never happened; left freedpeople dependent on white landowners
  • Negotiated labor contracts — refused to work in plantation gangs; preferred family sharecropping
  • Built political organizations — voted in large numbers; helped elect Republican governments in Southern states
Need a starting point?
"Freedpeople shaped Reconstruction by actively…"
Let's Go Deeper
Radical Reconstruction gave Black men the right to vote and hold office — but it lasted less than 10 years before being crushed. Why did it fail to create lasting change?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare Presidential and Radical Reconstruction. Which approach do you think was more likely to create real, lasting freedom for Black Southerners — and why?

Standard 1.03
The Reconstruction Amendments
Think About It
Three amendments were passed in five years that fundamentally changed what the Constitution meant. What do you think it took to make that happen — and why were those changes so hard to keep?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
"The First Vote," Harper's Weekly 1867 — Black men in the South exercised the right to vote for the first time. Within a generation, white supremacist violence and voter suppression laws would strip most of them of that right. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Reconstruction-era South — the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments transformed the constitutional landscape during this period. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'The First Vote,' Harper's Weekly 1867 — Black men in the South exercised voting rights for the first time. Within a generation these rights would be stripped away by violence and law. Library of Congress.
13th Amendment (1865)
  • Abolished slavery throughout the United States — permanently
  • Exception clause: "except as a punishment for crime" — led to convict leasing, a new form of forced labor
  • Passed by Congress in January 1865 — required Lincoln to use significant political capital to secure the votes
  • Ratified December 1865 — after the war ended but before Southern states were readmitted
Need a starting point?
"The 13th Amendment was a landmark because…"
14th Amendment (1868)
  • Defined citizenship: all persons born in the U.S. are citizens — directly overturned the Dred Scott decision
  • Equal protection clause: no state can deny any person equal protection of the laws
  • Due process clause: states cannot deprive persons of life, liberty, or property without due process
  • Became the legal foundation for most civil rights advances of the 20th century
Need a starting point?
"The 14th Amendment mattered most because its equal protection clause…"
15th Amendment (1870)
  • Guaranteed that the right to vote could not be denied based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude
  • Did not include women — led to a rift with some suffragists
  • Southern states found workarounds: poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and violent intimidation
  • Effectively suspended for most Black Southerners from the 1890s until the Voting Rights Act of 1965
Need a starting point?
"The 15th Amendment's promise was undermined because…"
Let's Go Deeper
The 14th Amendment promised equal protection under law in 1868. It took nearly 100 years and the Civil Rights Movement to begin enforcing that promise. Why do you think legal rights and actual lived reality diverged so dramatically?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain what each Reconstruction Amendment was supposed to do. Then think about the gap between constitutional promise and actual lived reality — why did it exist, and who was responsible for it?

Standards 1.04 & 1.05
Limitations on African Americans After Reconstruction
Think About It
After Reconstruction ended, a new system of racial oppression emerged in the South — not slavery, but something that tried to replicate its power. How did it work?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thomas Nast, "The Union as It Was," Harper's Weekly 1874 — Nast shows the KKK and White League working together, their hands clasped over a Black family forced to live in fear. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The post-Reconstruction South — as federal troops withdrew, Democratic 'Redeemer' governments stripped Black Southerners of rights gained during Reconstruction. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Thomas Nast, 'The Union as It Was,' Harper's Weekly 1874 — Nast exposes the organized terror campaign against Black Southerners that destroyed Reconstruction. Library of Congress.
Segregation & Jim Crow Laws
  • Jim Crow laws: state and local laws mandating racial segregation in virtually every aspect of life
  • Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): Supreme Court upheld "separate but equal" — legal foundation for segregation for 58 years
  • Separate schools, restrooms, restaurants, train cars, hospitals — enforced by law and violence
  • Poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses: stripped Black voters systematically
Need a starting point?
"Jim Crow laws created a system of segregation that…"
Violence & Intimidation
  • Lynching: an estimated 4,000 Black Americans lynched in the South between 1877 and 1950
  • KKK terrorism: organized violence to prevent Black political participation and economic advancement
  • Race riots: white mobs destroyed prosperous Black communities — Tulsa (1921), Rosewood (1923)
  • Convict leasing: Black men arrested on minor charges and leased to businesses as forced labor
Need a starting point?
"Violence was used to maintain racial oppression because…"
Economic Oppression
  • Sharecropping: freedpeople became tenant farmers, forever in debt to white landowners
  • Debt peonage: laws made it illegal to leave a farm while in debt — trapped families for generations
  • Exclusion from labor unions: most major unions refused Black members
  • Great Migration began: Black Americans eventually left the South by the millions to escape oppression
Need a starting point?
"Economic oppression kept Black Southerners trapped because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Reconstruction gave Black Americans legal rights — but within a generation those rights were stripped away. How was that possible, and who was responsible?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain how the system of racial oppression after Reconstruction worked. What were its components, and how did they reinforce each other? Then think about why it lasted so long.

Standard 1.06
Life for Native Americans After the Civil War
Think About It
While Black Southerners fought for equal rights, Native Americans faced a different kind of assault — on their land, their sovereignty, and their very identity. What do you think was most devastating?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Indian Territory, Maxwell 1889 — by the late 19th century, most Native nations had been confined to a fraction of their original lands. Library of Congress, Geography and Map Division.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Sioux boys on arrival at the Carlisle Indian Industrial School, 1879 — photographed before forced assimilation. The school's motto: 'Kill the Indian, save the man.' Library of Congress.
Wars & Removal
  • Post-Civil War: U.S. Army launched systematic campaigns to confine Plains Indians to reservations
  • Battle of Little Bighorn (1876): Sioux and Cheyenne destroyed Custer's forces — last major Indian victory
  • Wounded Knee Massacre (1890): U.S. soldiers killed 250-300 Lakota Sioux — ended armed resistance on the Plains
  • Buffalo extermination: hide hunters killed 30–60 million buffalo by 1890 — destroyed the Plains nations' food supply
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. government used military force to remove Native Americans because…"
Reservations
  • Dawes Act (1887): broke up tribal lands into individual allotments — sold "surplus" land to white settlers
  • Reservations: Native nations confined to small portions of their original territories
  • Land loss: Native Americans held 138 million acres in 1887; 48 million by 1934 — lost 90 million acres in 47 years
  • Poverty: reservation lands were often arid and unproductive — deliberately chosen to be economically worthless
Need a starting point?
"The reservation system was devastating to Native communities because…"
Forced Assimilation
  • Carlisle Indian Industrial School (1879): children taken from families, forbidden to speak their languages, forced to adopt European dress and customs
  • "Kill the Indian, save the man" — Richard Pratt's motto for the boarding school system
  • Over 350 Indian boarding schools operated — children punished for speaking their native languages
  • Native religions banned: Ghost Dance outlawed; traditional ceremonies criminalized

Some historians call U.S. policy toward Native Americans a form of cultural genocide. What evidence would you point to for or against that argument?
