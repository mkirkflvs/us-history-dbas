Standard 3.01
Isolation, Intervention & Imperialism
Think About It
America was founded rejecting European-style empire — yet by 1900 it controlled territories across the Pacific and Caribbean. What changed?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
A political cartoon depicting America's expanding global presence, c.1900 — the U.S. had gone from a regional power to a Pacific imperial one. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
U.S. overseas territories, 1900 — the Spanish-American War transformed the U.S. into a Pacific and Caribbean imperial power virtually overnight. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Uncle Sam's expanding reach, c.1900 — American cartoonists captured the nation's dramatic transformation from a republic to an empire. Library of Congress.
Isolationism
  • Washington's Farewell Address (1796): warned against "entangling alliances" — set the tradition
  • Monroe Doctrine (1823): U.S. would stay out of European affairs; Europe stay out of the Americas
  • U.S. focused on building the nation at home through most of the 1800s
  • Example: U.S. refused to involve itself in European conflicts throughout the 19th century
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. practiced isolationism by…"
Intervention
  • Intervention: using military force in another country without full occupation or control
  • Roosevelt Corollary (1904): U.S. claimed the right to intervene in Latin American countries
  • Monroe Doctrine expanded — from "Europe stay out" to "U.S. can go in"
  • Example: U.S. sent troops to Cuba, Dominican Republic, and Nicaragua to protect American interests
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. moved from isolationism to intervention when…"
Imperialism
  • Imperialism: taking political and economic control of foreign territories
  • Alfred Thayer Mahan: argued U.S. needed a powerful navy and overseas bases to be a world power
  • Economic imperialism: American businesses invested heavily in Cuba, Hawaii, and the Philippines
  • Spanish-American War (1898): marked America's full entry into imperial competition — gained Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico
Need a starting point?
"Industrialization drove the U.S. toward imperialism because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Was American imperialism fundamentally different from European imperialism — or was it the same thing with a different name? What would you point to in the evidence?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through how the U.S. went from Washington's warning against entanglements to controlling territories across two oceans. What pushed that change?

Standard 3.02
The Spanish-American War
Think About It
Cuba was fighting for independence from Spain — so why did the U.S. get involved? Was it really about Cuban freedom, or something else?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
"Remember the Maine!" — the explosion of the USS Maine in Havana Harbor, February 15, 1898, killed 266 sailors and inflamed American public opinion. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Spanish-American War, 1898 — fought in two oceans simultaneously, the four-month conflict made the U.S. a colonial power. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'Remember the Maine!' — the USS Maine exploded in Havana Harbor, February 15, 1898, killing 266 sailors. Yellow press blamed Spain; the cause remains disputed. Library of Congress.
Why the U.S. Got Involved
  • Yellow journalism: Hearst and Pulitzer newspapers sensationalized Spanish atrocities in Cuba to sell papers
  • USS Maine explosion (Feb 1898): 266 Americans killed — newspapers blamed Spain despite unclear cause
  • De Lôme Letter: Spanish diplomat insulted President McKinley — leaked to press, inflamed American opinion
  • Economic interests: American businesses had $50 million invested in Cuba's sugar industry
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. entered the Spanish-American War because…"
What Happened
  • Declared April 1898 — lasted only 113 days, called "a Splendid Little War"
  • Battle of San Juan Hill: Theodore Roosevelt's Rough Riders charged Kettle Hill — made Roosevelt a national hero
  • Commodore Dewey destroyed the Spanish fleet at Manila Bay, Philippines, in a single morning
  • Spain sued for peace in August 1898 — U.S. had won decisively in both theaters
Need a starting point?
"The Spanish-American War was fought in two places simultaneously — in Cuba and…"
Consequences
  • Treaty of Paris (1898): Spain gave U.S. Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico — U.S. paid Spain $20 million
  • Cuba became nominally independent — Platt Amendment gave U.S. the right to intervene at will
  • Philippine-American War followed (1899–1902): Filipinos fought U.S. occupation — 200,000+ Filipino casualties
  • U.S. emerged as a global power; Theodore Roosevelt rose to national prominence
Need a starting point?
"The consequences of the Spanish-American War revealed that the U.S. had become…"
Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. fought Spain claiming to liberate Cuba and the Philippines — but then refused to grant the Philippines independence. How do you reconcile that? Liberation or imperialism?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the causes, events, and consequences of the Spanish-American War. Was the U.S. motivated by genuine idealism, self-interest, or both? Make a case.

Standard 3.03
Causes of World War I
Think About It
Four years of the bloodiest war in history — triggered by the assassination of one man. What had to already be in place for one assassination to start a world war?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Europe in 1914 — divided into the Triple Alliance and the Triple Entente, with every major power bound by treaty to defend the others. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
European alliances, 1914 — the Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente meant any local conflict could automatically trigger a world war. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Europe divided, 1914 — every major power was bound by treaty to defend the others. When Austria-Hungary mobilized against Serbia, the dominoes fell within weeks. Library of Congress.
Militarism & Alliances
  • Militarism: European powers had been building massive armies and navies for decades
  • Arms race: Germany building a navy to challenge Britain created intense rivalry
  • Triple Alliance: Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy vs. Triple Entente: France, Russia, Britain
  • Alliance system meant a conflict between any two countries would automatically drag all six major powers into war
Need a starting point?
"The alliance system made WWI more likely because…"
Imperialism & Nationalism
  • Imperial competition: European powers competed for colonies in Africa and Asia — repeatedly creating crises
  • Nationalism: intense pride in one's nation — especially dangerous in ethnically diverse empires
  • Pan-Slavism: Russia backed Slavic peoples against Austro-Hungarian rule — direct conflict
  • The Balkans: Serbia's growing power threatened Austria-Hungary — the "powder keg of Europe"
Need a starting point?
"Nationalism and imperialism contributed to WWI by…"
The Spark
  • Archduke Franz Ferdinand assassinated in Sarajevo, June 28, 1914 — heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne
  • Assassin: Gavrilo Princip, Serbian nationalist — Austria blamed the Serbian government
  • Austria issued an ultimatum to Serbia; Russia mobilized to defend Serbia
  • Within weeks: the alliance system pulled all six major powers into war — Europe was at war by August 1914
Need a starting point?
"The assassination of Franz Ferdinand triggered WWI because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Some historians argue WWI was inevitable given the system of alliances, militarism, and nationalism. Others say it was a series of mistakes. Which argument is more convincing to you?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain the MAIN causes in your own words. Which one do you think was the deepest underlying cause of the war — and why?

Standard 3.03
From Neutrality to War
Think About It
Europe was destroying itself — and the U.S. watched from across the Atlantic for nearly three years before joining. What finally changed that?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
"I Want YOU for U.S. Army" — James Montgomery Flagg's iconic 1917 recruiting poster. Once the U.S. entered WWI, it needed to build an army quickly. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The Western Front, WWI — millions died for a few miles of ground. The sheer horror of this war shaped American reluctance to get involved. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'I Want YOU for U.S. Army' — James Montgomery Flagg's 1917 recruiting poster. Once Germany's Zimmermann Telegram was published, public opinion shifted and Congress declared war. Library of Congress.
Why the U.S. Stayed Out
  • Large German and Irish immigrant populations opposed entering on Britain's side
  • Wilson's "Peace Without Victory" — believed U.S. could mediate and end the conflict
  • Economic: U.S. sold war materials to all sides — neutrality was profitable
  • Progressive movement focused on domestic reform, not foreign wars
Need a starting point?
"The U.S. stayed neutral from 1914 to 1917 because…"
What Changed
  • Lusitania sinking (May 1915): German submarine sank British liner — 1,198 killed including 128 Americans
  • Germany resumed unrestricted submarine warfare (1917) — threatened any ship near Britain
  • Zimmermann Telegram (1917): Germany secretly proposed a military alliance with Mexico against the U.S. — British intercepted and published it
  • Russian Revolution (1917): Tsar overthrown — Wilson could now frame the war as democracy vs. autocracy
Need a starting point?
"The event that finally pushed the U.S. toward entering the war was…"
The Decision to Enter
  • Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war on April 2, 1917
  • The world must be made safe for democracy — Wilson's case to Congress
  • Selective Service Act (1917): drafted nearly 3 million men — largest military mobilization in U.S. history
  • Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918): made it illegal to criticize the war or the draft
Need a starting point?
"Wilson's argument for entering WWI was that…"
Let's Go Deeper
Was U.S. entry into WWI inevitable once Germany resumed submarine warfare, or were there other paths Wilson could have taken? What would you have done in his position?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through why the U.S. stayed neutral and then explain what finally changed. What do you think was the real turning point — not just the official reason, but the actual one?

New Technology & WWI's Impact
Think About It
WWI introduced technology that made killing industrially efficient. How do you think soldiers felt going into a war with 19th-century tactics against 20th-century weapons?

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab

◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American soldiers in a WWI trench wearing gas masks, 1918 — chemical warfare was one of WWI's most terrifying innovations, requiring entirely new protective equipment. Library of Congress.
New Technologies
  • Poison gas: chlorine and mustard gas caused horrific injuries — introduced a new dimension of terror
  • Machine guns: made frontal infantry attacks suicidal — forced armies into trenches
  • Tanks (first used by British, 1916): designed to break through trenches — unreliable early on but revolutionary
  • Airplanes: began as observation tools, evolved into fighters and bombers — changed warfare permanently
Need a starting point?
"New technology in WWI changed warfare by making it…"
Impact on Minority Groups
  • African Americans: 380,000 served — in segregated units; most served as laborers, not combat soldiers
  • 369th Infantry "Harlem Hellfighters": fought with French forces — received French military honors but segregated at home
  • Great Migration began during WWI: Black Americans moved north for factory jobs left vacant by soldiers
  • Native Americans served at higher per-capita rates than any other group — many as code communicators
Need a starting point?
"WWI affected minority groups in the U.S. by…"
The American Home Front
  • War Industries Board: government directed industrial production — total war economy
  • Liberty Bonds: Americans loaned money to the government to fund the war
  • Women filled industrial and office jobs as men went overseas — strengthened the suffrage argument
  • Espionage and Sedition Acts: Socialist Eugene Debs imprisoned for an anti-war speech
Need a starting point?
"On the American home front, WWI changed daily life by…"
Let's Go Deeper
WWI was supposed to be the "war to end all wars." Why did it fail to prevent future conflict — and what conditions did it actually create?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the new technologies and what the war experience was like for different groups of Americans. What do you think changed most about American life as a result of WWI?

Wilson's 14 Points & the Peace
Think About It
Wilson went to Paris believing he could build a lasting peace on fair principles. He came back with a treaty he barely recognized. What went wrong?
Historical Image

Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Europe after Versailles, 1919 — new nations emerged, empires dissolved, Germany lost 13% of its territory and was saddled with $33 billion in reparations. Library of Congress.
◈ Political Cartoon
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Woodrow Wilson on tour for the League of Nations, 1919 — he collapsed from exhaustion and suffered a stroke before the Senate voted. The U.S. never joined. Library of Congress.
Wilson's 14 Points
  • Self-determination: peoples should choose their own governments — end to empires dividing ethnic groups
  • Freedom of the seas and free trade — end to secret treaties between nations
  • Reduced armaments — nations should not maintain massive standing armies
  • League of Nations: an international organization to peacefully resolve future conflicts
Need a starting point?
"Wilson's 14 Points were idealistic because they called for…"
The Treaty of Versailles
  • War guilt clause (Article 231): Germany forced to accept sole blame for the entire war
  • $33 billion in reparations: Germany required to pay — crippled the German economy for years
  • Germany lost 13% of its territory and all its overseas colonies
  • German military limited to 100,000 men — stripped of its great-power status and dignity
Need a starting point?
"The Treaty of Versailles differed from Wilson's 14 Points because…"
The League of Nations
  • Wilson's greatest priority — an international body to prevent future wars
  • Article 10: member nations would collectively defend each other against aggression
  • Senate opposition led by Henry Cabot Lodge — feared loss of sovereignty and entanglement in foreign wars
  • U.S. never joined — fatally weakened the League; it lacked the power to enforce its decisions
Need a starting point?
"The League of Nations failed to prevent future conflict because…"
Let's Go Deeper
Some historians argue the Treaty of Versailles planted the seeds for World War II by humiliating Germany. Do you agree? What evidence would you point to?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare Wilson's vision with what the treaty actually became. Why was there such a gap between the two — and what were the long-term consequences of that failure?
