Standard 3.01
Why America Built an Empire
Think About It
America was founded by people who left an empire. So how did it end up building one of its own just 100 years later?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Uncle Sam's expanding reach, c.1900 — American cartoonists captured the nation's transformation from republic to empire. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Ellis Island, early 1900s — even as America was absorbing millions of immigrants at home, it was acquiring colonies abroad. Two visions of American expansion. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
These cartoons normalized imperial expansion by depicting it as natural, even heroic — the same visual logic used by European colonial powers. Library of Congress.
For most of the 1800s, the U.S. stayed out of foreign entanglements. George Washington had warned against them. But by the 1890s, American factories were producing more than Americans could buy, and businesses needed new markets. Suddenly, the same logic that had built European empires started making sense to American leaders too.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Ideology
Social Darwinism and the "White Man's Burden" gave imperialism a moral-sounding justification: America wasn't conquering, it was civilizing. Cartoons and politicians sold it that way.
›
Step 2
The Strategy
Naval theorist Alfred Thayer Mahan argued the U.S. needed overseas bases and a powerful navy to be a world power. Hawaii, Guam, the Philippines, all fit the strategy.
›
Step 3
The Contradiction
A country founded on anti-colonial revolution was now running colonies. Filipino revolutionaries who'd fought Spain had to fight America next. 200,000+ Filipino civilians died.
🔗
Real World Connection
The argument that powerful countries should intervene in weaker ones "for their own good" didn't end in 1900. That logic justified Vietnam, Iraq, and a dozen smaller interventions. Worth asking whether it ever actually worked.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The United States moved toward imperialism because... even though..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Was American imperialism really different from European imperialism, or was it the same thing with a better marketing campaign?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain how the U.S. went from isolation to empire. What changed, and what did the U.S. gain and lose in the process?

Standard 3.02
The Spanish-American War
Think About It
A newspaper publisher once reportedly told his illustrator: "You furnish the pictures, I'll furnish the war." Can media actually start a war?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'Remember the Maine!' — the explosion killed 266 sailors. Nobody proved Spanish responsibility, but that didn't slow the march to war. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American soldiers in action — the Spanish-American War was called 'a splendid little war' by those who didn't fight it. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The visual language of imperialism — depicting conquest as civilization, and subjugated peoples as grateful for American rule. Library of Congress.
The Spanish-American War lasted 113 days and made the U.S. a world power. It started partly because of genuine concern for Cuban independence, partly because American businesses had $50 million invested in Cuba, and partly because newspaper moguls like Hearst and Pulitzer found that outrage sold papers. The USS Maine exploded in Havana Harbor, probably an accident, and suddenly America was at war.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Media
Yellow journalism turned every Spanish action in Cuba into an atrocity. The Maine explosion was immediately blamed on Spain, without evidence. Public opinion shifted overnight.
›
Step 2
The War
It was called "a splendid little war." 113 days, two theaters (Cuba and the Philippines), American victories everywhere. Theodore Roosevelt became a national hero at San Juan Hill.
›
Step 3
The Empire
The Treaty of Paris gave the U.S. Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. Then the U.S. fought a brutal three-year war to prevent Philippine independence. 200,000+ Filipino civilians died.
🔗
Real World Connection
Think about how social media can accelerate public anger about something before the facts are clear. Yellow journalism in 1898 and viral misinformation today operate by the same principle: outrage spreads fast, corrections don't.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Spanish-American War showed that the United States was willing to... even when..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
The U.S. fought Spain to "liberate" Cuba and the Philippines, then refused to let the Philippines be free. How do you square that?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Walk through the Spanish-American War, what sparked it, what happened, and what the consequences were for American foreign policy.

Standard 3.03
How One Assassination Started a World War
Think About It
One man was shot in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914. By August, every major European power was at war. How is that even possible?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Europe divided, 1914 — every major power was bound by treaty to defend the others. When one mobilized, they all had to. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
WWI trench life — the alliance system that was supposed to prevent war instead guaranteed that any local conflict would become a continental one. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Two wars, two generations — the Civil War veteran and his WWI grandson epitomize how quickly new forms of catastrophic warfare replaced old ones. Library of Congress.
World War I didn't happen because of one assassination, that was just the match. The gunpowder had been building for decades: massive armies, a web of alliances that meant everyone had to fight if anyone fought, intense national rivalries, and an arms race nobody knew how to stop. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand just lit it all at once.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Setup
Europe had been arming for decades. Germany was challenging British naval supremacy. Every major power was bound by secret treaties to defend the others. The system was a trap.
›
Step 2
The Spark
Archduke Franz Ferdinand was shot by Serbian nationalist Gavrilo Princip. Austria blamed Serbia. Russia backed Serbia. Germany backed Austria. France backed Russia. Britain backed France. War.
›
Step 3
The Horror
Machine guns, poison gas, and 400 miles of trenches made this unlike any previous war. 20 million dead. The "war to end all wars" actually planted the seeds for a worse one.
🔗
Real World Connection
Group chats have exploded over something small because of a chain reaction, one person said something, someone else reacted, and suddenly everyone was dragged in. The WWI alliance system was the world's worst group chat.

"WWI started because a single event triggered a chain reaction that nobody could stop, specifically..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Some historians say WWI was inevitable given the alliance system. Others say it was a series of mistakes. Which argument do you find more convincing, and why?

Standard 3.03
How America Got Dragged Into WWI
Think About It
Europe was destroying itself, and the U.S. watched for almost three years before jumping in. What finally changed?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
'I Want YOU for U.S. Army,' 1917 — once the U.S. entered, it needed to build an army from scratch and fast. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
The span of American wars — it took three years for the U.S. to enter WWI after the Civil War generation still lived. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
WWI trench life, 1917–18 — the horror of modern industrial warfare shaped American reluctance to enter the conflict. Library of Congress.
Wilson won reelection in 1916 on the slogan "He kept us out of war." Five months later, Congress declared war. The shift came from a combination of German submarine warfare killing American civilians, a secret telegram proposing that Mexico attack the U.S., and Wilson's belief that he could shape the peace if America was at the table.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Reluctance
Large immigrant populations opposed the war. The progressive movement was focused at home. Neutrality was also profitable, the U.S. was selling supplies to both sides.
›
Step 2
The Tipping Points
The Lusitania sank in 1915, killing 1,198 including 128 Americans. Then Germany sent the Zimmermann Telegram proposing a military alliance with Mexico against the U.S.
›
Step 3
The Entry
Wilson asked Congress for war on April 2, 1917. The Selective Service Act drafted 3 million men. The Espionage Act made it illegal to criticize the war. Free speech had its limits.
🔗
Real World Connection
The Zimmermann Telegram was a decoded German message published in newspapers. Think about what it would take today for public opinion to flip completely on a foreign policy question, sometimes it's just one revelation.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The U.S. stayed neutral until... because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Wilson said the U.S. entered WWI to "make the world safe for democracy." Did it work?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Explain why the U.S. stayed neutral for so long and what finally pushed it into the war. What do you think was the real turning point?

Standard 3.05
What WWI Actually Did to People
Think About It
Machine guns, poison gas, bombers, tanks, all invented or perfected for WWI. What does it do to soldiers when killing becomes industrial?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
American soldiers in a WWI trench, 1918 — gas masks, artillery, and machine guns made this unlike any previous war. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Immigrants in New York — the Great Migration accelerated dramatically during WWI as Black Americans moved north for factory jobs left by soldiers. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Flagg's poster became one of the most recognized images in American history — the power of a direct address to the viewer. Library of Congress.
WWI introduced technologies that made mass death efficient for the first time. It also sent Black Americans to fight for a democracy that denied them rights at home, brought millions of women into the workforce, and used federal law to imprison people for opposing the war. The home front and the battlefield were both transformed.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Technology
Poison gas, machine guns, and artillery barrages that lasted for days made the trench warfare of WWI uniquely horrific. Soldiers developed "shell shock", what we now call PTSD.
›
Step 2
The Contradiction
380,000 Black Americans served in a segregated military, mostly as laborers. They returned from fighting for democracy abroad to face Jim Crow at home. The Double V campaign began here.
›
Step 3
The Home Front
The War Industries Board controlled production. Women filled factory jobs. The Espionage Act criminalized dissent. Eugene Debs went to prison for a speech opposing the draft.
🔗
Real World Connection
The Great Migration. Black Americans moving north for factory jobs, accelerated dramatically during WWI. Cities like Chicago and Detroit were transformed. The demographic shifts that shaped modern American culture started here.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"WWI changed daily life in America by... especially for..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
Black Americans fought for a democracy that denied them rights. Does military service create an obligation for a country to extend full citizenship? What does history suggest?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Think about the WWI home front experience for different groups, women, Black Americans, immigrants, anti-war activists. Pick one group and explain what WWI meant for them.

Standard 3.07
The Peace That Made the Next War
Think About It
Wilson traveled to Paris believing he could build a just and lasting peace. He came home with a treaty that helped cause the next world war. What went wrong?
Historical Image
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Wilson on his 1919 tour to build support for the League — he collapsed from exhaustion before the Senate voted. The U.S. never joined. Library of Congress.
Map
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
Reconstruction after the Civil War and Versailles after WWI shared the same fatal flaw: punitive peace that stored up the energy for the next conflict. Library of Congress.
◈ Primary Source
🖼Click to open imageopens in new tab
WWI trench — the war's horror was so extreme that European leaders wanted to punish Germany severely enough that it could never start another one. They achieved the opposite. Library of Congress.
Wilson's 14 Points were idealistic and genuinely ambitious, self-determination, freedom of the seas, a League of Nations to resolve future conflicts peacefully. But France and Britain wanted revenge and reparations. The Treaty of Versailles humiliated Germany so completely that it created exactly the resentment Adolf Hitler would later exploit.
How It Connects
Step 1
The Vision
Wilson's 14 Points called for national self-determination, no secret treaties, disarmament, and an international organization, the League of Nations, to prevent future wars.
›
Step 2
The Reality
The actual treaty blamed Germany for everything, demanded $33 billion in reparations, stripped territory, and limited the German military to 100,000 men. It was designed to humiliate.
›
Step 3
The Consequence
Germany's economy collapsed under reparations. The humiliation fueled resentment. By 1933, Hitler was using Versailles to justify everything. Will Dyson drew it in 1919: the "1940 Class" weeping.
🔗
Real World Connection
Have you ever seen a conflict "resolved" in a way that just made the other side angrier and more determined? That's Versailles. Punitive peace doesn't create stability, it stores up the energy for the next explosion.
🤔 Need a starting point?
"The Treaty of Versailles failed as a peace settlement because..."
◈ Let's Go Deeper
A British political cartoonist drew the Treaty of Versailles in 1919 and labeled the crying child "1940 Class." He was right. What does it mean that ordinary people could see this coming, but the leaders couldn't, or wouldn't?
Your TurnPut it in your own words.
Compare Wilson's vision with what the treaty actually became. Why was there such a gap, and what were the consequences of that gap?
